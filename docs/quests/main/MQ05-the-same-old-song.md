# MQ05 — The Same Old Song

## Introduction

The player walks into the Chantry mid-toll — the same hour, the same hymn, rung without
a break since the Stall, a whole cathedral-town keyed to one endless note. There is no
gate to earn here and no monster to corner: the Hierophant sits untouchable at the heart
of the organ-colosseum, and stays untouchable for exactly as long as his harmony holds.
The quest is a lesson in that harmony. The Fool learns the hymn's meter by attending the
service that teaches it, redirects the town's choir-pipes to loosen the music, silences a
stray bell-Blank in the square to prove the sky won't fall if the song breaks — and only
then can fight, landing hits in the off-beats the sabotage opens, to strict, audio-
telegraphed timing (with visual beat markers for accessibility). There is one soft,
mechanically weightless choice along the way: whether to coax the young chorister Linnet
into a note of her own. Unbinding the Hierophant fires `WS_HIEROPHANT_UNBOUND` — the
bells fall silent for the first time in three centuries, then learn new songs, and
weddings and festivals resume across every settled region. MQ05 has no requirements and
may be played in any order in Act I; if the Fool reaches the Chantry after MQ13 (Death,
"An Ending"), the marked scenes below carry their `[If CONFESSED: …]` variants — by then
everyone in the Chantry knows what a visit from the Fool finally costs.

### EXT. THE CHANTRY: The Lychgate — THE SACRED HOUR

so that the hymn seems to rise out of the town rather than be sung by it.

### INT. THE CHANTRY: The Nave, the Endless Service — THE SACRED HOUR

Inside, the service is always in progress and never in conclusion. Rows of choristers
hold a chord that resolves and re-begins without a seam; the congregation sits in the
long patience of people who stopped waiting for the end so long ago they've forgotten
there was meant to be one. It should be oppressive. It is, instead, unbearably gentle —
which is worse.

**THE FOOL**

**CHORISTER LINNET**
> He's *certain*. He'd tell you that's the same thing. Some mornings I even believe him.

### EXT. THE CHANTRY: The Rooftops and Bell-Towers — THE SACRED HOUR

Above the nave, the town's music is fed by choir-pipes — great copper throats mounted
along the ridge-lines and the bell-towers, each piping one held voice into the endless
hymn. Three of them can be reached and turned: redirect a pipe and its voice bends out of
true, thinning the harmony that keeps the middle untouchable.

**[Traversal + light-stealth loop, repeated three times. Each choir-pipe is guarded by a

**THE QUERENT** *(first pipe)*
> There's the shape of it. Turn the pipe on the gap, not the note — same as the child
> told you. Get greedy and clang about on the beat, and that bell-headed fellow rings the

> spot; rude not to listen.

As each pipe turns, its held voice sours a half-step, and — very faintly, town-wide — the

chorister falters and recovers.

**THE QUERENT** *(third pipe turned)*
> Three down. Hear that? The whole town's a hair off true now and hasn't the first idea
> why. That wobble is every door you're about to walk through. Go and prove it in the
> square before you climb to the middle — no sense learning to swim in the deep end.

### EXT. THE CHANTRY: The Town Square — THE SACRED HOUR

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The square sits under the shadow of the colosseum drum. With the harmony loosened, one
bell-Blank has come unmoored from the hymn entirely — its cracked handbell tolling a
half-beat behind everything else, staggering in slow circles, ringing an alarm nobody
answers because the note is *wrong* and the town has no word for wrong. Passers-by drift
around it, uneasy, unable to name what troubles them.

Linnet arrives at the square's edge, drawn by the off-note like everyone else, but unlike
everyone else knowing exactly what it is.

**CHORISTER LINNET**
> That's — that shouldn't be possible. A bell out of true, ringing on and on and nothing
> struck down for it. Go on. *Please.* Silence it. I have to see it silenced and the sky
> stay up. I have to see it with my own eyes or I'll never believe the middle can break.

**[Set-piece fight: one bell-Blank, silenced by striking on the off-beats it now rings
in. Audio telegraphs its every swing; the accessibility beat-pulse marks each. This is
the fight's mechanics rehearsed at single-enemy scale — the proof, to the player and to
Linnet both, that the harmony breaks without catastrophe.]**

The Fool lands the silencing blow. The bell-Blank does not shatter dramatically — it
simply stops, sits down in the square like a marionette with its strings gone slack, and
its cracked handbell rolls away ringing nothing at all.

The town holds its breath. The sky stays up. The other bells toll on, untroubled, over a
square that has just watched a piece of its eternal music be switched off — and survived
it.

**CHORISTER LINNET** *(barely a whisper, radiant)*
> Nothing ended. Something *stopped*, and nothing ended. Those aren't the same. Nobody
> ever told me those weren't the same.

**THE FOOL**
> Now you tell everyone else.

**CHORISTER LINNET**
> ...I might. When the song's free, I might tell the whole square. Go up, then. Go and
> stop the biggest bell of the lot.

### INT. THE CHANTRY: The Organ-Colosseum — THE SACRED HOUR

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The doors of the colosseum stand open — they have never once been shut, because no one
who understood the hymn ever thought to try to enter and end it. Inside is a great round
of tiered stone seating with no audience, ringing a floor of inlaid staves, and at the
centre, raised on the console of an organ grown into the very walls, THE HIEROPHANT — mitre,
worn cope, both hands laid on the manuals — conducts the eternal hymn without looking up,
because in three hundred years nothing has ever arrived worth looking up for.

He is untouchable. The player can walk the whole ring and find no opening; every strike
slides off the held harmony like rain off a roof.

**THE HIEROPHANT**
> A newcomer. On the staves themselves. You have walked into the middle of the Rite,

**THE FOOL**
> The song's already wobbling. Did you hear?

### CUT SCENE — the hymn breaks

The Fool reaches the master pipe at the colosseum's heart and turns it on the final off-
beat. The great voice bends, cracks, gutters — and the whole hymn, town-wide, three
centuries deep, breaks off mid-note.

Silence.

Not a pause. Not a held rest waiting to resolve. *Silence* — genuine, total, the first
the Chantry has known since the Stall. Somewhere far below, a congregation that has not
heard silence in three hundred years makes a sound like a whole town flinching.

The Hierophant's hands lift off the manuals as though the keys have gone cold. He looks
at the office around him — the mitre, the cope, the endless certainty — and it splits, top
to bottom, with a sound exactly like an organ pipe rupturing: a long tearing overtone and
then nothing.

**THE HIEROPHANT**
> There is a gap. There was always a gap. And in the gap there is—

He draws a breath to name it. And the breath brings up a name that is not doctrine's — a
name surfacing mid-inhale, the way a drowning man's head breaks water:

**THE HIEROPHANT**
> ...*Bede.*

He says it as if he has caught it before it could get away again. He tries it a second
time, gentler, to be sure it holds.

**BEDE**
> Bede. That's — that was mine. Before the hour. Before I was a *when* instead of a
> *who*. I had a name and I set it down to carry the office and I never — nobody told me
> I could pick it back up.

He steps down off the console a smaller man than sat at it — not beaten, not fallen,
simply *unburdened*, the mitre gone crooked and no longer minded. He looks at the Fool
without a scrap of doctrine in his face, humbled and, underneath it, appallingly relieved.

**BEDE**
> [If CONFESSED: I know what you are. I sang against it as long as I had a song. And now
> that it's quiet enough to think, I find I'm not afraid of the ending. I'm afraid of how
> *tired* I've been. Three hundred years of holding one note. Let someone else hold it.
> Or let it fall. Either. Either is a mercy I forgot the shape of.]
>
> First new note in three hundred years, and it turns out to be a *name*. Mine. You gave
> an old shepherd back the one thing his own hour took off him. That's — that is a better
> sermon than I ever preached.

He lifts a card from the folds of the cope — no flourish, no rite, just a man handing over
what is finally, simply, his to give.

**BEDE**
> Trump V. Rite, if anyone writes it down kindly. Blessings, and a circle nothing unwelcome
> may cross, and one litany for when your last petal's spent. Take it. A rite's only ever
> been a kindness people forgot to stop performing. Perform it better than I did.

### CUT SCENE — Brother Tolliver, the one it wasn't good for

Away from the square, in the emptied nave, BROTHER TOLLIVER stands where the choir used to
hold its chord, hands lifted to warm at a fire that has gone out. The new peal reaches him
faintly through the stone, and he cannot make his face do anything with it.

**BROTHER TOLLIVER**
> I knew the hour. Do you understand me — I *knew* it. When to rise, when to kneel, when
> to hope and when to let hoping be. Sixty years and then three hundred more, and every
> one of them I knew exactly where I was in the song.

He listens to the fumbling new peal. It is joyful. It is not his.

**BROTHER TOLLIVER**
> Listen to that. Flat as a dropped plate, and off the beat besides. Sixty years I'd have
> had a novice scrubbing the crypt for that. *(a small, helpless, wet laugh)* And now it's
> the sound of everyone being happy, and me being wrong to mind. There's a joke in there.
> I was never any good at the jokes.

He wipes his face with the back of a robed wrist, and the certainty does not come back.

**BROTHER TOLLIVER**
> And now there's a new song, and everyone's so *pleased*, and I don't know when to kneel
> any more. What do I pray *by*, if the hour won't tell me? How does a man keep faith with
> a clock that's allowed to be wrong?

[If CONFESSED, his grief shifts register — no longer the loss of routine alone:]

> [If CONFESSED: And they tell me it's all ending, besides — the whole Reading, the whole
> world, when your Fool's done turning cards. So it isn't only my hour that's over. It's
> *the* hour. The last one. I built a life on a thing that would hold forever, and it
> turns out forever was the lie and the ending was the mercy, and I'm too old to learn to
> be glad of it. I'll try. Gods turn my card, I'll try. But not today.]

He does not curse the Fool. He is not owed a resolution and does not get one. He simply
lifts his hands again to the cold air where the chord used to be, and holds them there.

# MQ09 — The Light on the Mountain

## Introduction

The player enters the Dim already knowing its one rumour: a lantern moves on the far
slopes every night, and nobody has ever caught it. There is no boss waiting in an arena
here — the mountain itself is the ordeal, cold and dark and built to be read, not fought.
The player's whole job is wayfinding: track cold embers and half-buried footprints up a
scree slope, cross a star-blind valley by landmark and by Pip's nose because there is
nothing overhead to steer by, and keep going past every cairn where earlier followers
turned back. The light stays one ridge ahead the entire time — not by magic, but because
no one in three hundred years had followed far enough to be worth stopping for. Catching
the light is not the victory. Being caught catching it is. When the Fool finally outlasts
the pattern, the Hermit is sitting by his fire with two cups already poured, and asks the
Fool a single question over tea — one whose answer the world files away and hands back,
much, much later, in other people's mouths. He gives up the lantern like it weighs a
mountain, because it does. The quest can be played at any point; the Dim's dusk is the
Hermit's own doing, not the Sun's, so even a day/night world stays gloomy here until the
light comes down. Its one warm room is Widow Fenner's waypoint shack, and its one small
grief is hers: found at last, the Dim needs a great deal less finding.

## QUEST: THE LIGHT ON THE MOUNTAIN

### EXT. THE DIM: The Lowland Trailhead — DUSK (PERMANENT)

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The path up from the lowlands crosses a treeline and simply stops climbing into daylight.
Above the last stunted pines the whole mountain lies under a blue-grey dusk that never
deepens to night and never lifts to morning — a held breath of a sky, cloudless and
starless both, giving the eye nothing to fix on. The cold here is the patient kind, the
kind that has all the time it needs. Underfoot, frost that has never once melted.

[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND: the turning sky reaches even the Dim now — a real evening tries to
gather at the ridgelines — but the mountain keeps its own gloom regardless, a dusk the
moving sun can't quite lift. It will lift when the light comes down, and not before.]

Far off, on a slope the eye can barely make out, a single warm point of light moves. It
travels a ridgeline at a walking pace, unhurried, and goes behind a shoulder of rock, and
does not come out the other side.

**THE QUERENT**
> There it is. The famous light. Three hundred years it's walked those ridges, and three
> hundred years nobody's laid a hand on it. Folk come up here meaning to. Folk go home
> again. The mountain's very good at sending people home.

**[Tutorial-adjacent prompt: no waypoint marker appears. The Almanack objective reads
only "Follow the light." The Dim does not hold the player's hand; that is the region.]**

Pip presses close against the Fool's leg, not frightened — this is not the Mirrormarsh —
just cold, and glad of the company, breath smoking.

### EXT. THE DIM: Widow Fenner's Waypoint Shack — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

A little further on, a light that *doesn't* move: a low stone shack with a lit window and
smoke standing straight up from its chimney in the windless cold. A Waystation shrine
stands beside it under a lean-to, its basin swept clear of frost by a hand that plainly
does it daily. The White Rose stirs as the Fool nears it.

The door opens before the Fool can knock. WIDOW FENNER stands in it — small, straight-
backed, a shawl pinned at the throat and a ladle still in one hand — and looks the Fool
over the way she has looked over every cold and lost thing to climb this path for longer
than she can rightly count.

> cold and no kettle.

> You've the look of one who's chasing the light. They all have that look. Sit down first;
> the light's not going anywhere it hasn't been going for three hundred years.

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE

Inside: a single room built entirely around warmth. Cots along one wall, more than one
person could ever need — because more than one person has always needed them. A long
table worn pale. Every mug on the shelf is a different mug, left by a different traveller,
kept clean against a return.

**WIDOW FENNER**
> Fenner. I keep the waypoint. Have done since — well. Since keeping it was worth doing.
> You warm your hands, and I'll tell you the one thing everybody comes up here to ask.

**THE FOOL**
> The light.

**WIDOW FENNER**
> The light. It walks the far ridges every dusk — which is every hour, up here, dusk being
> all we get. Never the same slope twice. Never close enough to hail. Folk set out after
> it bright as buttons, and the mountain walks them in circles till the cold talks sense
> back into them, and they come here, and I feed them, and they go down home.

She sets a mug in front of the Fool without asking. It is warm all the way through.

**WIDOW FENNER**
> That's the trick nobody credits. The light doesn't run. It just keeps *one ridge* on.
> Always one ridge. And one ridge is exactly as far as a body can bear to be from a warm
> fire before they turn round. Clever, that. Cruel, maybe. I've stopped deciding which.

END CUT SCENE

> Go on, then, since you've the look. Take the dog. Read the ground. And when the cold

### EXT. THE DIM: The Scree Slope — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The ground tilts up into a long fan of loose grey scree, treacherous underfoot, running
with the faint smell of old smoke. Someone has come this way — recently, and often.

**[Prompt: call Pip's Seek. Pip noses out a trail the eye would miss: cold fire-embers
buried in the scree, a line of them, each long dead, and between them half-filled boot-
prints pressed into frost that never thaws to erase them.]**

The Fool tracks the embers up the slope — reading the land, not a marked path: which way
the prints lean, where a hand steadied a fall, where the ash sits thickest. At the top of
the fan, over a lip of rock, the light shows for the first time up close: the warm point
of a walking lantern, a hundred yards off across a gully, held low by a figure the dusk
won't quite resolve. It is *right there.*

The Fool crests the lip — and the light is already on the next ridge across the gully, no
faster than a walk, going away. Not fleeing. Just keeping its one ridge.

**THE QUERENT**
> Ah. You felt that, did you. The little lurch of it being close and then not. Everyone
> feels that one. It's the mountain's opening move, and it has never once failed to work.
>
> Don't chase the light itself. You can't. Chase the *ground* it walked. That, you can
> read.

### EXT. THE DIM: The Star-Blind Valley — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The gully opens into a wide, flat valley floored in fog to the knee, walled by ridges on
every side that all, in the flat blue dark, look exactly like one another. Overhead:
nothing. No star, no moon, no sun — a lid of dusk with no mark on it anywhere to steer by.
Halfway across, a traveller would have no way of knowing which wall they came in through.

**[Navigation puzzle, no combat: the valley cannot be crossed by sight. The player
navigates by ground-landmark — a leaning spire of rock, a cairn, a frozen stream's line —
and by Pip. Send Pip ahead with Seek; he casts for the lamp-oil scent on the fog and
holds point in the correct direction, a living compass where the sky gives nothing.]**

[If WS_STAR_UNBOUND without WS_HERMIT_UNBOUND: even with the Mere's star returned to the
wider night, the Dim's own lid stays blank — the star-blindness is *this* mountain's
stasis, and it lifts only when its own light comes down. The valley is exactly as
trackless as it has always been.]

The light appears twice while the Fool crosses — once dead ahead, drawing them true, and
once off to the side down a false spur that ends in a drop. Reading the ground, not the
light, is the whole of the crossing. Trust the sky and the mountain keeps you. Trust the
dog and the dirt and it lets you through.

**THE QUERENT**
> Nothing up there to help you tonight. Nothing up there most nights. Whoever's carrying
> that lamp picked the one corner of the world where a light is the *only* thing worth
> looking at. That's either despair or it's an invitation. I've a guess which.

### EXT. THE DIM: The Cairn Field — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Beyond the valley the slope is studded with cairns — dozens of them, hand-stacked stones
of every age, some fresh, some slumped with centuries. Not grave-markers. Turning-points:
each one marks where a follower, once, decided this was far enough, and built a little
monument to the deciding before walking back down to Fenner's fire. Some have a scrap of
cloth tied to them. One has a child's shoe.

Among the cairns, a LOST TRAVELLER sits wrapped in three coats, cheerful as a robin,
adding a stone to a cairn of their own.

**LOST TRAVELLER**
> Building mine up proper this time. You after the light? Don't. It's a ghost, that. Old
> lamplighter died on his round and never noticed, still walks it, still lighting what's
> already lit. My gran said so. My gran was *rarely* right, but she was right about that.

**THE FOOL**
> How do you know it's a ghost?

**LOST TRAVELLER**
> Well, nobody's caught it, have they. Living things get caught. Only a ghost keeps its
> distance three hundred years. *(pats the cairn, fondly)* And a ghost you can stop
> chasing without feeling a fool about it. Handy, a ghost. Very restful.

A beat — the traveller looks at their own cairn, and the cheer thins by exactly one shade.

**LOST TRAVELLER**
> ...Built this the first time forty years back. Rebuild it every time I come up and lose
> my nerve. Suppose the cairn's the thing I actually finish. That's *something*, catching
> a thing you can finish.

[If CONFESSED: the traveller adds, quieter, not quite meeting the Fool's eye —]

> They say you're the one who's ending things. Region by region. *(a slow breath)* Funny.
> I've spent forty years not finishing one climb, and here's you finishing the whole
> world. I don't know whether to envy you or build you a cairn. Bit of both, maybe.

### EXT. THE DIM: The Tightening — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Past the last cairn — the highest one anyone ever built, the furthest anyone in three
hundred years ever came before turning round — there are no more cairns. Only unmarked
ground, and the light, closer now than it has ever let anyone see it. The Fool follows it
across three linked ridges, and something changes: the light is not gaining distance any
more. It walks; the Fool walks; the gap holds, then closes a hand's width, then a stride.

**[No new mechanic: this is the accumulation of everything the chase taught — reading
lean of ground, trusting Pip over the sky, not stopping. The region stops being the boss
here because the player has finally learned to read it faster than it can hide.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> Look at that. It's *slowing*. No — you're not gaining because you're faster. You're
> gaining because you didn't stop. That's the whole difference. That was always the whole
> difference. Nobody ever failed to catch that light. They only ever failed to keep
> walking toward it.

A pause. The light waits at the crest of the next rise — actually waits, the first time it
has ever done anything but recede — as though whoever holds it has looked back and seen,
for the first time in three centuries, someone still coming.

**THE QUERENT** *(the one wink)*
> Three hundred years, and the one who followed all the way turns out to be the card that
> can't be told where to stop. *(a dry breath)* I'd say I planned that, little Excuse. But
> we both know who's actually been doing the walking, and it isn't me.

### EXT. THE DIM: The Hermit's Fire — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Over the last rise, in a hollow out of the wind, a small fire burns. Beside it, on a flat
stone, sits the lantern — set *down*, for the first time in three hundred years, its light
steady and enormous up close, warm enough to feel. And beside the lantern sits a man.

THE HERMIT is old the way a mountain is old — unbothered by it. A traveller's cloak worn
to the weave, a face gone gentle and remote from a very long time spent thinking. He does
not rise, does not startle, does not run. On the flat stone, two cups of tea sit steaming.
Two. Poured, and waiting, and just cool enough to have been poured a little while ago — by
someone who knew, at last, that company was coming.

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE

**THE HERMIT**
> There you are. Sit — the second cup's gone cold about forty times over the years, but I
> keep pouring it. A lantern's no use to a man alone. It's for being *followed* by. And in
> three hundred years, do you know — *(he says this without a grain of self-pity, as a
> simple curiosity)* — nobody once followed far enough to be worth the pouring.

Pip sits, and leans against the Hermit's knee, entirely at ease. The Hermit rests a hand
on the dog's head like it is the most natural thing in the world.

**THE HERMIT**
> Not that the light was ever hidden. Anyone could have caught it. It only ever asked one
> thing — that a body keep walking after it when the fire behind them said turn round. Turns
> out that's the rarest thing there is. Rarer than courage. Courage is loud. This is just...
> not stopping.

**THE FOOL**
> Why keep walking away, then, if you wanted catching?

**THE HERMIT**
> *(a long, kind pause)* Because the walking was the question. And I wanted to see who'd
> answer it. *(he lifts the second cup, holds it out)* Drink. I've earned a conversation,
> and so, plainly, have you. Only — before the tea goes cold a forty-first time — let me
> ask you the one thing I climbed all the way up here to ask, and never had anyone to ask
> it *of.*

END CUT SCENE

a thing he has been holding for three hundred years:

**THE HERMIT**
> What have you been looking for, all this way?

| The place where all of this ends. | *(a slow breath; he looks into the fire)* ...Yes. I thought it might be that, for the one who made it this far. It usually is. Hold onto it gently. A thing like that will carry you, or it will carry you off. |

### CUT SCENE

The Hermit drinks his tea. It is, at last, warm. He looks at it, and at the Fool, and at
the dog, and something in him settles that has not settled in three centuries.

**THE HERMIT**
> There. I've been asked after, and answered, and had my tea hot. *(he sets the cup down,
> gently, finally)* That's the whole of what a lantern was ever for. I don't think I need
> to keep walking any more.

He reaches for the lantern. And here is the strange thing, the thing the Fool feels rather
than sees: as his hand closes on the handle, the lantern grows *heavy* — impossibly heavy,
a small warm light that suddenly weighs like the whole dark mountain it has crossed and
crossed. He lifts it anyway, both hands, and as he does the office cracks — the endless
walking, the always-one-ridge-on, three hundred years of being a light and never a man —
comes apart around him with a sound like ice giving on a pond.

And underneath it, surfacing the way a held breath finally lets go, a name.

**THE HERMIT**
> ...*Ellery.*

He says it carefully, turning it over, the way you'd test frost to see if it will bear
weight.

**ELLERY**
> Ellery. Yes. That was — that *is* mine. I'd gone so far into the thinking I forgot there
> was a thinker with a name. *(he looks up, and he is only an old man now, tired and glad)*
> Well caught, my friend. Not the light. Me. You caught *me*, which is the harder thing and
> the only one that counted.

He holds the lantern out across the fire — both hands, arms trembling with the mountain-
weight of it, offering it not as a prize won but as a burden he is finally, gratefully
allowed to set into someone else's keeping.

**ELLERY**
> Trump the Ninth. The Lantern. It's yours now — it was always going to be whoever kept
> walking. Hold it high when the dark lies to you; it doesn't know how to lie back. And
> when you find the ridge you've been walking toward all this way — *(a small, mountain-old
> smile)* — I hope there's someone sitting at it with the tea already poured.

The Fool takes the lantern. It is, in their hands, only a lantern again — warm, and light,
and easy to carry. The weight was never in the lamp. It was in the carrying it alone.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. THE DIM: The Slopes — AFTER

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The change comes over the mountain the way dawn would, if the Dim had ever been allowed
one. The lid of dusk thins, and *pricks*, and fills — stars, hundreds of them, then
thousands, laid across a sky that has shown nothing at all for three hundred years. For
the first time, the valleys are not star-blind. There is something overhead to steer by,
and nothing left up here that needs steering to.

[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND: the stars arrive into a sky that already turns — they take their
places among a real night, and the Dim's held dusk breaks at last into an ordinary,
merciful dark-and-dawn like everywhere else.]

[If WS_STAR_UNBOUND: these are not the Mere's single impossible star but the Dim's own
returned host — and up the slope a lamplighter stops dead, looks from one sky to the
other, and says only, *"...so that's where the rest of them were keeping."*]

Down every trail, small warm lights begin to move — the lost, coming down from the hills
at last, following the stars home the way they never could follow the one lantern. They
pass the Fool without quite understanding what changed, only that the mountain has, for
the first time in living memory, let them go.

### EXT. THE DIM: Widow Fenner's Waypoint Shack — AFTER

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The shack, when the Fool comes back down, is emptying. The cots are being stripped by their
own occupants, folk shrugging into coats and out the door with a nod, headed for the newly-
findable path home. By the time the Fool reaches the door, the long table sits nearly bare.
Widow Fenner stands among the different mugs, drying one that no longer has anyone to come
back and claim it.

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE

**WIDOW FENNER**
> Well. There they go. All of them, near enough. Down the mountain like water that finally
> found its slope. *(she sets the mug on the shelf, carefully, in its place)* Three hundred
> years I've been the warm window on a cold mountain, love. Turns out the mountain's not so
> cold any more. Turns out it doesn't much need a window.

She says it without bitterness. That is the worst of it — there is no one to be cross with.
The Fool has done nothing but good, and it has cost her the whole shape of her days.

[If CONFESSED: **WIDOW FENNER**

### CUT SCENE

Widow Fenner fills the kettle and sets it back over the fire, though the shack is empty and
the mountain is emptying and there is, by any sensible reckoning, nobody left to warm.

**WIDOW FENNER**
> Old habit. *(she catches the Fool watching, and lifts her chin, half a dare and half a
> hope)* Well — in case someone's still finding their way. You never do know. And a body
> likes a purpose. Even a made-up one. *Especially* a made-up one.

The kettle begins, very quietly, to sing. Outside, the new stars hold steady over a
mountain that has finally, after three hundred years, arrived somewhere.

END CUT SCENE

MQ09 ends here. The Dim's dusk is lifted, its stars returned, its lost brought down; the
Fool carries the Lantern, and Widow Fenner keeps a kettle on for no one, in case.

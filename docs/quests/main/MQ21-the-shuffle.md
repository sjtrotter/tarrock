# MQ21 — The Shuffle

## Introduction

The Axis has been open since the moment the player landed in the Spread; the lone figure
dancing at its center has been visible, too far to see clearly, from every region of the
game. This quest begins the instant the player crosses into the wreath of white stone —
at hour two or hour forty, with zero Trumps or all twenty. Nothing gates it and nothing
stops it but the fight itself: every Arcana still bound lends the Dancer their office as a
full phase, so what the player walks into scales exactly to the journey they did or
didn't take first. There is no Trump to win here — the World's card is not carried, it is
*turned*. This quest owns all three endings ([`narrative.md`](../../design/narrative.md)
§Endings): the True Shuffle (all 21 unbound), the Early Shuffle (any still bound), and the
Refusal (walk away at the final choice). The player who reaches this with all 21 Arcana
freed will, and only here, learn who the Querent has been all along, hear their own
Reading read back to them, and be handed the game's last input. The Fool is not the
world's savior. The Fool is its ending — and this is where the world, at last, finds the
courage to hear it.

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Threshold — STATE-DEPENDENT

The approach to the Axis runs in from the Hollows' terraces and the wreath-order's
outer rim, past the last of the telescope-makers' workshops and the worn standing-spot
where the rim-watcher keeps his post — all of them observing, as they always have, from
exactly as far away as they've ever dared. None of them follow the Fool in. That ground
is the Fool's alone, and everyone on the rim knows it without being told.

Ahead, the wreath: a ring of white stone, weather never touching it, the ground going
pale and level toward a still silver center where — near enough now to be a person and
not a distant rumor — a single figure turns, and turns, and does not stop.

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Wreath Line — TIMELESS

CUT SCENE

Pip does not hesitate at the threshold. He never has here. He pads across the wreath line
ahead of the Fool with the mild, unbothered ease of a dog who has done this exact walk
more times than anyone has bothered to count, and does not once look back to check that
he is followed.

The Querent has narrated every step of this journey — every region, every road, every
question at every edge. As the Fool crosses the line, they are, as ever, mid-thought:

**THE QUERENT**
> That's the last of the rim behind you now. Past here it's only the two of you and the
> one who's been — 

And they stop.

Not a pause for effect. The voice simply is not there any more, cut off between one word
and the next, the way a hand comes off a string. The silence where the Querent should be
is total, and it is the loudest thing in the game. No music but the distant, half-heard
dance rhythm that has haunted the Axis from every horizon since the first hour, still
never resolving, still nowhere quite loud enough to place. No weather. No barks. Nothing
but the turn of two feet on pale stone, and a small white dog walking calmly toward the
center of everything.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Approach — TIMELESS

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The wreath is an amphitheater, though there is no audience and never has been — a long
curved sweep of white stone falling away toward the dance floor at its heart. Set into
its inner walls, spaced evenly around the whole ring, are twenty-one alcoves: one for
each Major Arcana, in card order, the Fool walking the sequence of their own journey as
architecture before the fight ever makes it mechanical.

**[The alcoves read the player's save, one per Arcana:]**

- **[Each unbound Arcana's alcove]** holds its card, face-up, at rest — the corrupted,
  looping motif of that region finally resolved and quiet here, the card no longer stuck
  but *finished*, laid down like a hand played out.
- **[Each still-bound Arcana's alcove]** stands hollow and humming — an empty niche with
  the wrong, waiting pressure of a held breath, the office it should hold still out there
  in the world, still frozen, still lending its weight to the figure at the center.

**[Tutorial/world note: the ratio of filled to hollow alcoves *is* the player's ending,
told in stone before a blow is struck. A True Shuffle player walks a ring of twenty-one
resting cards. An Early Shuffle player walks past a ring that is mostly hollow, humming,
unfinished — and the fight ahead will make every one of those hollows a phase.]**

**[If all 21 unbound: the twenty-one cards, read left to right around the ring in card
order, are also — from where the Fool now stands — laid out in the order they were
turned; the alcoves quietly double as the Reading the Querent will lay on the floor
shortly. The player is looking at their own playthrough. Nothing says so yet.]**

The approach is long and it is meant to be. There is nothing to fight here, nothing to
pick up, nowhere to turn aside. Only the walk, and the alcoves, and the dance getting
slowly nearer.

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Dance Floor — TIMELESS

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

At the heart of the wreath, on a floor of the same pale stone worn faintly circular by
three hundred years of the same steps, the Dancer turns. Close, now. Close enough to see
what the telescope-makers ground four generations of lenses trying to see and never quite
could: not a symbol, not a rumor — a person. Travel-worn clothes under the wreath's
silver light, the cut of them not so different from the Fool's own. And at the edge of
the dance floor, set down long ago and never taken up again, a bindle — a traveler's
bundle on a quarterstaff, the twin of the one across the Fool's own shoulders — and
beside it, on the stone, a single white rose, withered to paper, three petals closed and
long since done opening.

The Dancer does not stop to speak. They cannot; that is the office, and the office has
held them since the last Shuffle. But they can talk while they turn, unhurried, in the
tired, kind, dry voice of someone who has had three centuries to get used to their own
company.

### CUT SCENE

**THE DANCER**

> expectant, the way the whole Spread has been)* I felt you coming a long way off. You
> get so you can feel a Fool approaching. It's the one thing that ever changes out here.

**THE DANCER**
> Three hundred years. *(a dry, worn breath of a laugh)* And I never once learned a
> second dance. You'd think I'd have used the time. You'd think, of all the things a body

## QUEST: THE OFFICES BORROWED — FIGHT, PART ONE

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Dance Floor — TIMELESS

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[This entire sequence plays only if one or more Arcana remain bound. A fully-unbound
save (all 21) skips part one entirely and goes straight to THE DUEL below — the Dancer
has nothing left to borrow. See `arcana.md` §XXI.]**

Per [`arcana.md`](../../design/arcana.md) §XXI, the fight scales to the journey: one full
phase per still-bound Arcana, in the order of the card sequence (I, then II, then III…),
each office lent to the Dancer in compressed form. Each phase opens the same way — the
matching hollow alcove flares along the wreath, its unresolved motif surging up out of
the near-silence for exactly as long as the phase lasts, and the Dancer takes up that
office's silhouette: the Magician's three-shell misdirection, the Emperor's unbreakable
guard, the Chariot's charge, Death's long scythe-reach, the Tower's collapsing arena, and
so on down whatever list the player left unfinished. As each borrowed office is beaten,
its alcove goes dark and quiet again — not filled, not finished, just *spent* — and the
next hollow one along the ring flares to take its place.

**[Combat note: the borrowed movesets are the compressed forms specified per Arcanum in
`arcana.md`; the compression budget per office is set at combat-prototype time. The BotW
rule holds absolutely — a 20- or 21-phase gauntlet at hour two must be genuinely
*possible*, survivable by the brilliant, never balanced to the player's level. This is
the Early Shuffle: legendary for challenge runners, brutal on purpose, and never once
scolding the player for attempting it. See Open questions.]**

The Dancer fights each borrowed office with a borrowed body's economy — no flourish, no
taunting, only the tired precision of someone wearing a coat that was never theirs. They
do not speak during the borrowed phases. There is nothing of *them* in these movements;
that is the point. The player is not fighting the previous Fool yet. They are fighting
the unfinished world, one frozen office at a time, worn like armor by the one soul still
holding all of it up.

When the last hollow alcove goes dark — when there is nothing left in the world to borrow
— the borrowed weight sloughs off the Dancer's shoulders, and for the first time the
figure at the center stands as only themselves.

**[All part-one branches converge here, at the start of THE DUEL. A fully-unbound save
arrives here directly.]**

## QUEST: THE DUEL — FIGHT, PART TWO

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Dance Floor — TIMELESS

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

With nothing left to borrow, the finale becomes what `arcana.md` §XXI and
[`narrative.md`](../../design/narrative.md) §Endings always promised: two Fools, and no
gimmick. No adds. No arena trick. No rotating hazard. The deliberate rhyme with MQ13's
candle-ring — the purest fight in the game, played out one more time, and the last time.

But where Mortimer fought with his own office and his own scythe, the Dancer fights with
the Fool's *own move vocabulary*: Bindle forms the player will recognize from their own
hands, the same dodge, the block-step, the read-and-punish, and — unmistakably, when the
player leaves a window — the Dancer's own Fool's Chance, slow-time and all, turned back
against them. This is the mirror the Anti-Fool faked in MQ18 (`main/MQ18-the-path-that-
lies.md`) made, at last, *sincere*: that fight was an illusion wearing the player's build;
this one is the real thing, a Fool who truly fights like a Fool because they truly are
one — were one, walked this same journey, took this same office, and never got to put it
down. There is no reversed Trump here, no borrowed cruelty. Only two people who belong to
no position, meeting in the one place the whole Reading was ever pointed at.

**[Combat note: the Dancer carries no Trump — the World's card is not carried (`arcana.md`
§XXI) — so the duel is base-vocabulary only: Bindle, dodge, block-step, Fool's Chance. It
is tuned as a skill duel to rhyme with MQ13's difficulty-peak intent, beaten clean, with
nothing to exploit and nothing needed. Two Fools dancing.]**

When the last opening comes, it is the Fool's to take. The Dancer does not fall like
someone struck down. They come to a stop — and stopping, after three hundred years of not
being allowed to, is the whole of it. The turning ends. The worn-circular floor holds one
final, unrepeated step, and then stillness, and the distant dance rhythm that has haunted
the Axis since the first hour of the game finally, finally resolves — one long-held phrase
coming to rest, and the largest use in the game of the unbinding stinger: one enormous
card turning over, scaled past every seismic turn before it, because it is the last.

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE — THE YIELD

The office cracks. It comes apart the way it did off Mortimer — not shattering, but
thawing, the weight of being *The World* rather than a person melting off and away — and
the Dancer stands there in the settling quiet, smaller and plainer and unbearably still,
a traveler at the end of a road that went on three centuries too long.

Somewhere under the crack, a name surfaces — the same way it surfaced for the Bataleur,
for Mortimer, for every office broken along the whole journey: the held breath of a name
finally allowed to be said in the first person, the *I* and the name permitted to sit
together in one mouth for the first time in three hundred years.

The Dancer does not say it to the Fool.

They kneel — slowly, gratefully, a body remembering how — and they take Pip's scruffy
head in both hands, and they lean in close, and they say their name to the dog. Only to
the dog. The words are too low for the camera, too low for the Fool, too low for the
player, and they are kept that way deliberately and forever: whatever the Dancer's name
is, whoever they were before they were The World, it is spoken once, into a white dog's
ear, and the game never gives it to us, because it was never ours to hear.

Pip wags.

That is the whole of it. Pip wags, tail sweeping the pale stone, and the freed Fool — the
one who was The World a moment ago and is only a tired person now — laughs, wet-eyed, at
whatever a dog's tail can say back.

**THE DANCER**
> *(to Pip, the only thing said aloud, and it is enough)* Aye. That's me. That's still me.
> After all this. Fancy that.

They get to their feet — steady, unhurried in a way that is suddenly *choice* rather than
office — and look at the Fool properly for the first time, showman to showman, Fool to
Fool.

**THE DANCER**
> Right, then. Before we finish it, there's the one thing I have to ask you, same as it
> was asked of me, my time round. And you have to answer it standing up.

A beat. The wreath holds its breath.

**THE DANCER**
> Do you know what turning it does?

## QUEST: THE READING — *[True Shuffle path only]*

### EXT. THE AXIS: The Dance Floor — TIMELESS

CUT SCENE — THE READING

For the first time since the wreath line silenced them, the Querent speaks again — and
the return of the voice, after so long a quiet, is its own event. Gentle. Unhurried. No
wink, no deflection, none of the old sliding-round-it.

Around the ring, the twenty-one alcoves give up their cards — each one lifting free of its
niche and drifting down onto the pale floor between the two Fools, laying themselves out
left to right not in card order now but in the order the *player* turned them, across the
whole playthrough, from the first region they freed to the last before this one. The
Fool's Reading, dealt across a hundred hours without the player ever being told they were
dealing it, laid bare on the stone at the center of the world.

**THE QUERENT**
> There. Your reading, little Excuse. You've been dealing it since the cliff — one card a
> region, in your own order, and no one before you ever dealt it quite the same. I couldn't
> read it to you until it was whole. It's whole now. So let me read you three.

**[The recital reads exactly three positions from the player's `READING_ORDER`
(`world.md` §Global states) — never the whole 21, never a branch, only a *styling*
(`narrative.md` §The Fool's Reading, design guardrail: the Reading styles, it never
branches). The full set is a 21-cards × 3-positions = 63-line matrix; the template below
plus three worked examples per position are authored here, and the remaining 54 lines are
authored at content pass. The three positions are fixed:]**

- **First card turned** — *how the world woke*. Styles the ending's opening image and the
  dawn of the new deal.
- **Eleventh card** *(the exact middle of 21)* — *the heart of the journey*. Styles the
  Querent's reading of who this Fool was.
- **Last card before The World** — *the world's final lesson*. Styles the tone of the
  gathering-up and the final farewell.

**Recital template** *(per read position; the bracket resolves to the player's card in
that slot):*

> [Position frame] — [the card in that slot]. [One reading sentence, in the Querent's
> voice, tuned to that card's traditional meaning *and* to that position's function.]

**Worked examples — FIRST CARD TURNED (*how the world woke*):**

> First card — the Magician. So your world woke to a trick laid bare, and learned in the
> same breath that a wonder shown honestly is a wonder still. That's the dawn you've dealt
> the next one: a waking that is its own first act of making.

> First card — the Sun. So your world woke all at once, into a light it had forgotten was
> ever *allowed* to move. The next Fool will open their eyes on a morning that means to go
> somewhere. It always shows in the dawn, the first card. Yours will be gold.

> First card — Death. So your world woke by learning, first of all, to *end* — the
> hardest lesson taken up front, and every waking after it made gentler for the taking.
> The new deal's morning will carry that mercy folded into it, whether it ever learns the
> name or not.

**Worked examples — ELEVENTH CARD (*the heart of the journey*):**

> Eleventh card, the heart of it — the Chariot. So at your middle you were a thing in
> motion, that could not be steered and would not stop. That's who you were, little
> Excuse, when the going was hardest: forward, and forward, and forward.

> Eleventh card, the heart of it — Justice. So at your center you were a weigher of things
> left too long unsettled. You carried the scales into the very middle of your own road,
> and you did not tip them for anyone. I saw. I always see the middle card clearest.

> Eleventh card, the heart of it — the Star. So at your core you were hope with its feet
> gone wet — the small, stubborn light a body keeps beside them in the dark and does not
> let the wind have. That is who you were when it counted most.

**Worked examples — LAST CARD BEFORE THE WORLD (*the world's final lesson*):**

> Last card before this one — Death. So the world's last lesson to you was the plainest
> it owns: that an ending is a mercy, and the gathering-up you're about to do is nothing
> to be afraid of. It taught you the ending *last*, on purpose, so you'd turn this card
> unafraid. Kind of it. Kind of you, to learn it.

> Last card before this one — the Moon. So the world's last lesson was that the final fog
> lifts, and the things you dreaded in the dark were only lost folk wanting home. The
> gathering-up will be gentle, then, and honest, and clear of every illusion.

> Last card before this one — the Sun. So the world's last lesson was daybreak — that even
> an ending can come warm, and gold, and feel more like a morning than a night. The
> gathering will carry that light out with it. A good last card, that. One of the kindest.

**[Matrix note: the other 54 position-lines (each remaining card read in each of the

The three cards the Querent read glow a moment brighter than the rest, and settle. The
Reading lies complete on the floor of the world.

## QUEST: THE ANSWER — *[True Shuffle path only]*

### EXT. THE AXIS: Looking Outward — TIMELESS

CUT SCENE — THE ANSWER

Immediately on the recital's close, still True Shuffle path only, the view lifts and turns
outward — away from the dance floor, out over the wreath's rim, and the whole Spread opens
below and around exactly as it did on the leap from the Cliff, only now every region of it
is *awake*: the Prestige's roads open, the Noonlands under a moving sun, the Stillmarsh
carrying its leaves at last, the Spire down, the stars out, all twenty-one regions lit and
living and — the game makes the player feel this before it says it — all of them turned
inward, all of them *listening*, toward the one still point they have circled since before
memory.

The Fool has been able to ask, since the very first edge on the very first cliff: *who
are you?* The Querent has slid round it every time, for a hundred hours, gently. They do
not slide now.

**THE QUERENT**
> You asked me once, on the cliff, who I was. I told you I was someone who'd waited a long
> while to have someone to talk to. That was true. It just wasn't *all* of it, and you've
> earned all of it now — every card of it, dealt and turned and read.
>
> Look out there. Go on. Every region you woke. All of it, listening.
>
> I'm not a voice in your ear, little Excuse. I never was. I'm the thing your ear is
> *inside of.* The Spread. The world. All of it. A reading gets dealt *for* someone — for
> the one who sat down and asked the question — and this reading, this whole three-hundred-
> year reading, was always being dealt for me. About me. *How does my story end?* That's
> the only question a querent ever asks. It's the only one I've ever asked.

The view holds on the whole living world, every horizon of it, quiet and attending.

**THE QUERENT**
> And I was *afraid* of the answer. That's the truth of the Stall — not a curse, not a
> catastrophe, no villain anywhere in it. Three hundred years ago I saw the last card
> coming and I flinched. I refused to hear how it ended. And a reading whose querent has
> stopped listening can't be dealt — no Fool could be dealt into it — so everything just…
> stopped. One card from the end. Golden, and airless, and pretending.
>
> You are here because I finally found the nerve to ask again. To sit back down at my own
> table and say the question out loud and *mean* it this time. That's what you are, little
> Excuse. You're not my saviour. You were never that. You're my *answer* — the one I was
> too frightened to hear, walked all the way up to me on your own two feet, so that this
> time I couldn't look away.
>
> The world has been asking how its story ends. *(a breath)* You're how it ends. And I
> asked for you. Standing up. At last.

END CUT SCENE

## QUEST: THE ENDINGS

### 10a. THE TRUE SHUFFLE — *[all 21 unbound; player turned the card]*

### EXT. THE AXIS → THE SPREAD, ENTIRE — TIMELESS

CUT SCENE

The Fool turns the card. The World comes face-up.

There is no fire. There was never going to be fire. The Shuffle is a *gathering-up* — the
world collected the way a dealer squares a spread at the end of a hand, gently, without
grief, because the hand was well played and it is time.

The regions fold closed one at a time, **in the exact order the player unbound them**
(`READING_ORDER`) — the world ending in the order it woke. Each region, as it folds, is
given one line of farewell drawn from the choices the player made there. The montage's
whole *tone* is styled by the last card the player turned before The World (the recital's
third read); its opening image and, later, the new deal's dawn are styled by the first
card they ever turned (the recital's first read).

**[The 21 farewell lines are a matrix: one per region × that region's branch states
(`world.md` §World-state matrix). Five worked examples across different branch states are
authored here; the remaining regions are authored at content pass to the same one-line,
folds-in-`READING_ORDER` staging. Each is short — a single held breath as a region closes
like a card laid down.]**

**Worked farewell — The Prestige (branch-dependent):**

> `[If WS_TROUPE_TRAVELING:]` The striped canvas comes down like a hand squared away.
> *"Road runs out of road at last. Best show we ever ran — and, wouldn't you know it, the
> very last. Goodnight, all. Goodnight."*
>
> `[If WS_TROUPE_SETTLED:]` The market square dims stall by stall. *"Lights out on the town
> we only just built. It was a good one, for the little while it got to be a town at all.
> Worth it. Every plank."*

**Worked farewell — The Stillmarsh (Death; no branch):**

> The lanterns go out one by one down lanes that finally, finally led somewhere. *"No
> rush, he always said. There never was. Sleep now, marsh — you of all places have earned
> the nothing."*

**Worked farewell — The Divide (branch-dependent):**

> `[If WS_DIVIDE_EASTMARRIED:]` The bridge closes like a book with the ribbon still in it.
> *"Married at last, and folded up the same week. But married. It counts. It always counted."*
>
> `[If WS_DIVIDE_WESTMARRIED:]` *"Two towns, one bridge, one wedding — and then the quiet.
> They got their day. Not every canyon does."*

**Worked farewell — The Undervault (Devil; deliberately unresolved):**

> The gilded pit dims, and some of its folk are walking up as it closes and some are
> walking back down, the way they always would. *"Some climbed out. Some chose the dark
> again. The card keeps both, and asks no one to choose twice."*

**Worked farewell — The Noonlands (Sun; the flagship world-change):**

> The gold fields dim under a sun that learned, at the very end, how to set. *"The first
> sunset was worth three hundred years of noon. This is the second one. Turns out it's
> just as good. Down you go, then. Down you go."*

**[Matrix note: the other 16 regional farewells — each keyed to that region's unbinding
and any branch flags it carries — are authored at content pass to this staging. Regions
the player unbound fold with a farewell; the fold order is `READING_ORDER`, always.]**

As the last region folds, the wreath is all that remains, and the two Fools on it, and
the dog.

The freed Fool — the one who was The World — crosses to the edge of the dance floor and
takes up the bindle they set down three hundred years ago, and lifts the withered white
rose from beside it, and holds it a moment, and does not look back at it again. They shoulder
the bindle. They look, once, at our Fool — and there is nothing owed in the look, no
warning, no torch passed with ceremony; only one traveler recognizing another at a
crossroads, and glad of them.

**THE DANCER** *(the last thing they say; not a name, never a name)*
> Mind the dog. He'll mind you. *(the almost-laugh, one final time, light as anything)*
> And learn a second dance, would you? I never did. You've the time now. All of it.

They walk to the rim of the Axis and step off the edge — not down, the way the Fool leapt
from the Cliff, but *up*, out and upward into the pale silver, the way a card lifts free
of a spread when the hand is done. Gone. Relieved. Home, at last, whatever home is for a
Fool.

Our Fool takes the center.

The worn-circular floor accepts one step, and another. And on the stone at the dance
floor's edge, where the old withered rose was lifted away, the White Rose the Fool has
carried since the Cliff — three modest petals — opens. All of it, at once, every petal, a
full slow bloom in the silver light: the one flower in the whole game that only ever
regrew, blooming now for the first and only time, because the journey is done and there is
finally nothing left to spend it on.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. A NEW CLIFF: A New High Meadow — BEFORE DAWN → DAWN

CUT SCENE

Black. No image. A sound like cards being squared against a table, once, twice — and then
the unmistakable snap-slide of a single card being dealt.

The new deal. A new world, dealt fresh. And the opening image — the light it comes up in —
is styled by the first card the player ever turned, the recital's first read: **[the dawn
of the new cliff takes its color and its weather from the player's first unbinding — gold
if the Sun, grey-then-clearing if the Moon, honest-and-plain if Death, and so on per the
matrix.]**

A new campfire, embers just gone grey. A new Fool lying beside it — not our Fool; a
stranger, dealt into a world with nothing to undo and nothing to remember, exactly as ours
once was. Somewhere in the meadow, a bindle leans against a stone. A White Rose lies loose
in the sleeping hand, three petals closed and modest.

And already awake, already sitting a few feet off, watching with the specific patience of
a dog who has been awake for some time and is not in any hurry about it, is Pip.

The same Pip. Not a grey hair on him. He does not bark. He does not fuss. He waits, tail
giving one polite thump against the grass, for the new Fool's eyes to open.

**[The persisting detail: one small thing from the player's own journey carries into the
new deal, so the loop reads as *closed* and *ours*. The strongest candidate is the whittled
wooden keepsake Pip digs up at the very start of MQ00 — the same small carved thing, tucked
into the new Fool's bindle or resting by the new campfire, the keepsake that was ours now
waiting for the next one. This is deliberately left as MQ00's open question: the persisting
detail resolves once MQ00's keepsake is locked, and this scene is staged to hold whatever
that keepsake turns out to be. See Open questions.]**

The new Fool stirs. Their eyes begin to open. Pip's tail thumps once more, patient as
stone.

The card is not shown to us. The waking is not shown to its end. The image holds on Pip,
watching a new Fool wake, at the start of a new journey that will end exactly the way this
one did, and be worth it exactly as much.

CUT TO CREDITS — over the Fool's journey theme, arranged one last time as it has never been
allowed to be arranged before: **resolved**. Not stuck, not looping, not corrupted. The one
throughline melody of the whole game, played all the way through, and finished.

END CUT SCENE

## Choices & branches

No hard `branches:` flag-pair: the ending is determined by save state (count of Arcana
unbound), not by a mutually-exclusive world-state choice. `WS_WORLD_UNBOUND` fires
identically for both Shuffle endings (10a/10b); the Refusal (10c) fires nothing and leaves
the quest re-enterable. The Fool's-answer dialogue at the yield (CONFESSED and NOT
CONFESSED tables, both first-pick-commits) is flavor only — it colors the exchange with
the Dancer and gates nothing; the fight, the yield, the choice, and the endings are the
same whichever line is picked.

## Note on the withheld name and the withheld line

Two things in this quest are withheld **permanently and by design**, and neither is an
omission to be filled at content pass:

- **The Dancer's freed name** is never heard by the player. Every other office in the
  game returns its holder's name aloud, first-person, at the unbinding (the Bataleur's
  *Wicke*, Death's *Mortimer*); this one does not. The Dancer says their name only to Pip,
  too low to hear, and Pip wags. The Dancer never pairs "I" with any name on screen. The
  game keeps the name forever. This asymmetry is the whole point of the beat: the previous
  Fool is *us*, one deal removed, and we do not get to name ourselves at the end — the dog
  does, because the dog is the one continuity across every deal, and the game never says
  so.
- **The Querent's final line before THE CHOICE** (the single reserved wink, the True
  Shuffle path only) is deliberately not drafted; it is held as a marked placeholder for
  the director's own pass. This is the one explicit, mandated exception to full-scripting
  in this file.

## Note on CONFESSED

This quest carries `[If CONFESSED]` / `[If NOT CONFESSED]` variants at the yield (the "Do
you know what turning it does?" exchange), keyed off MQ13 (`main/MQ13-an-ending.md`), the
origin of the global `CONFESSED` state. Note the structural implication: the True Shuffle
path (all 21 unbound) is *necessarily* CONFESSED, because all 21 includes Death — so the
Reading recital and the Querent's answer only ever play in a CONFESSED run. The NOT
CONFESSED variant is reachable only on an Early Shuffle run (Death still bound), and its
harder, kindness-starved tone carries straight into the 10b ending, exactly as the outline
staged.

# MQ15 — Terms and Conditions

## Introduction

The player descends into the Undervault — the only region with no sky — to unbind the
Devil, Old Nick Lowry, and finds the game's darkest comedy waiting at the bottom of a
gilded pit: a city where everyone signed for their own chains and will tell you,
cheerfully, that they are delighted with the arrangement. The heart of this quest is not
the fight but the *negotiation before it*. Old Nick receives the Fool at a beautiful desk
and offers three real deals — a cut of your coins, a petal of the White Rose, and Pip
waiting outside the gate — each buying a buff that works exactly as printed, each costing
exactly what it says. Sign none and the fight is brutal and proud; Old Nick will respect
that answer above any other. Sign all three and he barely needs to lift a hand, because
one clause in the print lets him borrow the Fool's own equipped Trumps and turn them
reversed against them. Every contract is honest. That is the whole horror. After the fight
the chains fall across the Undervault — and, per `world.md`, not everyone climbs out;
Merrow Slate walks partway up the stair and comes quietly back down. If the player reaches
the Undervault after MQ13 (Death, "An Ending"), the key scenes carry `[If CONFESSED: …]`
variants: Old Nick, who never lies, finds the Fool's larger purpose the most honest
transaction he has ever been offered.

### EXT./INT. THE UNDERVAULT: The Gate Stair — DUSK (LAST SKY)

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The way in is a long stone stair sunk into the western hills, and above it the last open
sky the Fool will see until they climb back out. A velvet rope, absurdly, cordons the
descent — gold-tasselled, a little frayed, the sort a theatre keeps to make a queue feel
chosen. Warm gaslight rises from below in place of daylight, the colour of butter and old
brandy. It smells of coin, and beeswax, and something sweet underneath that the Fool
can't name and isn't sure they want to.

**[Player action: descend the Gate Stair past the velvet rope. Pip trots at heel, game
as ever — he simply keeps one ear on the Fool the whole way down, the way a good dog
minds a companion in a stranger's house.]**

pauses at the rope without being told to.]

> Down we go, then. Mind the last of the sky on your way past — they don't keep any of it

### CUT SCENE

The stair opens onto a balcony over the pit entire: a gilded well of a city dropping away

### INT. THE UNDERVAULT: The Desk — GASLIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The door opens onto the pit's lowest and grandest tier: a floor of black-and-gold marble
ringed by the whole city looking down, and at its centre a beautiful desk. Not a throne —
a *desk*, mahogany and green leather, an inkwell, a fair stack of blank contracts squared
neat, a single pen laid across them at a considerate angle for a right-handed guest.

Behind it sits OLD NICK LOWRY — the Undervault's own residents say the name daily, an
epithet worn smooth by three centuries of custom — trim, unhurried, dressed like a man who
has never once been cold, with a face built for putting people at their ease. He rises the
exact correct amount when the Fool enters. He is charming, and he is funny, and he is
awful, and he manages all three in that order and without hurry.

At the foot of the desk's dais, Pip stops. His hackles lift. He growls — once, low, a
sound the Fool has not heard him make at anything else in the whole world — and then, at
a glance from the Fool, settles, watchful, unhappy. It is the only warning the game gives.

### CUT SCENE

**OLD NICK LOWRY**
> There you are. Do sit — no? Stand, then, by all means, a standing guest drives a
> harder bargain and I've no objection to a hard bargain. I so rarely get a *new* one.

He gestures, easy, at the whole gold pit above them.

**OLD NICK LOWRY**
> You'll have seen the residents on your way down. Happy, every one. I want you to hold
> that in your mind, because you're about to assume I did something dreadful to them, and
> I want the assumption gone before we talk. I never did a single thing to anyone here
> that wasn't written down first, read aloud, and signed atop. I don't cheat. I don't
> lie. I've never had the need. The truth closes more deals than a lie ever managed —
> that's the one secret of the trade, and I'll give it you for free.

**THE FOOL**
> And if I've come to fight you?

**OLD NICK LOWRY**
> Oh, we'll get there. We always get there. But you came down a very long stair, and it

He turns the top three contracts face-up, unhurried, and squares them.

**OLD NICK LOWRY**
> Read every word. I'll not stop you. I never stop anyone. Stopping people is the one

## THE NEGOTIATION

*The three clauses below are offered together, at the desk, in any order. **Each clause is
its own first-pick-commits choice** — Sign or Decline, confirmed, locked for the
encounter. The Fool may sign any number: all three, none, or any subset. Old Nick prints
every term in full and reads none of them wrong; the plain print is the whole of the
threat. Declining any clause costs nothing and offends him not at all. The Fool may ask
one short question per clause before deciding (the earnest/foolish option is preserved in
each set); the question does not commit.*

### THE COIN CLAUSE

Old Nick slides the first contract forward. The printed body reads, in full and in plain
hand:

> **THE COIN CLAUSE.** The signatory's fortune shall run richer for the coming
> engagement — Fortune generated increased by one half, in full, for its duration. In
> consideration whereof: an honest cut of the coin now carried on the signatory's person
> shall pass to the house, counted and taken at signing, and shall not be returned by any
> outcome of the engagement, win or lose. The sum is real. The loss is permanent. Signed
> in knowledge of both.

### CHOICE DIALOG — the Coin Clause *(one question may be asked; does not commit)*

| The Fool | Old Nick's response |
|---|---|
| How much of my coin? | An honest cut. A fair one — I'll show you the figure before you sign, and it won't change after. That's the part people never believe until it's true. |
| Is the coin ever refunded? | Never. Not if you win, not if you lose, not if you weep. It says so, there, in the ordinary-sized words. I do like to keep the important terms *large*. |
| What if I've no coin to spare? *(foolish)* | Then it's a poor clause for you, isn't it, and you should decline it, and I'll think none the less of you. I don't sell what a soul can't afford. Bad for repeat custom. |

### CHOICE OPTIONS — the Coin Clause *(first pick commits; confirm prompt; figure shown before confirm)*

| Choice | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Sign the Coin Clause. | The stated cut is counted from the Fool's purse on the spot and gone for good. For the encounter only, Fortune generation is increased by 50%. Old Nick: *"Pleasure. Genuinely. The coin's already mine, and you feel richer for it — that's the finest kind of sale there is."* |
| Decline the Coin Clause. | No cost, no buff. Old Nick: *"Sensible. Keep your coin. It fights better in your pocket than it ever did in mine, most likely."* |

### THE FLESH CLAUSE

Old Nick slides the second contract forward. He does not linger on it, which is its own
kind of tell. The printed body reads, in full and in plain hand:

> **THE FLESH CLAUSE.** For the coming engagement the signatory's White Rose shall blossom
> whole and carry one petal beyond its natural span — a fuller measure of mending than the
> Rose was ever grown to hold. In consideration whereof: one petal of the Rose's utmost
> capacity shall be surrendered to the house at signing. The Rose will bear one petal
> fewer thereafter. This is not a loan. Signed in knowledge that the Rose is smaller after.

### CHOICE DIALOG — the Flesh Clause *(one question may be asked; does not commit)*

| The Fool | Old Nick's response |
|---|---|
| A petal of the Rose — forever? | The Rose comes back smaller, yes. It says so plainly. I'd be a fraud to soften it, and I'm never a fraud. Only awful. |
| Why do you want a petal? | [the one honest beat] ...Do you know, I've stopped asking myself that. I collect what people will part with. The Rose parts easier than most. That should tell you something, and it never does. |
| Is my life worth one petal? *(earnest)* | That's the only question in the whole room worth asking, and it's the one clause I'll not answer for you. You'll price your own petals, or you'll leave them be. Either's an honest choice. |

### CHOICE OPTIONS — the Flesh Clause *(first pick commits; confirm prompt)*

|---|---|

For the encounter only, the Rose reblossoms full and carries +1 petal. Old Nick: *"There. Fuller now than it's ever been, and smaller forever after. Both true at once. That's the shape of every good deal, if you look."* |

### THE COMPANIONSHIP CLAUSE

Old Nick slides the third contract forward more gently than the others, and for the first
time all evening something moves behind the ease in his face — not guilt, he hasn't the
equipment for guilt, but something adjacent to it, a craftsman's respect for a cut that
lands. Pip, at the dais foot, growls again, low.

> **THE COMPANIONSHIP CLAUSE.** For the coming engagement a second shall stand at the
> signatory's side — a spectral echo bearing the likeness of the signatory's Present-slot
> Trump, casting as it casts, for the whole of the fight. In consideration whereof: the
> white dog waits beyond the gate. The gate is closed for the engagement's duration. The
> dog is not harmed, not held, not touched. He simply waits outside, and cannot be called,
> and can be heard. Signed in knowledge that the dog waits, and hears, and is heard.

**OLD NICK LOWRY**
> I'll be plain, since plain is all I do: that one hurts. Not the dog — the dog's fine,
> the dog will be *bored*, which for a creature like that is nearly an insult. It hurts
> *you*. That's the term. I don't touch him. I don't need to. I only shut a gate, and you
> fight the biggest fight of your life listening to him wait for you on the other side of
> it. Everything printed. Nothing hidden. The cruelty's entirely in the arithmetic.

### CHOICE DIALOG — the Companionship Clause *(one question may be asked; does not commit)*

| The Fool | Old Nick's response |
|---|---|
| Will Pip be hurt? | No. Read it — "not harmed, not held, not touched." I keep my terms in both directions. He waits, that's all. That's the whole of it, and it's enough. |
| Why take my dog at all? | I don't take him. I take the *comfort* of him. Different thing, and worth far more. A soul fights harder frightened and lonelier — but you'll do it with a spare pair of hands, so it's a fair swap on paper. |
| He'll wait the whole time? *(earnest)* | Every second. Right at the gate. He won't understand it and he won't stop. Dogs don't sign, you see. It's the one contract I've never once got a paw to. Believe me, I've offered. |

### CHOICE OPTIONS — the Companionship Clause *(first pick commits; confirm prompt)*

| Choice | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Sign the Companionship Clause. | Pip is put outside; the gate closes for the encounter (Pip is unharmed throughout — protection rule). A spectral second appears at the Fool's side and mimics the Fool's current Present-slot Trump for the whole fight. Pip can be heard, faintly, at the gate for the fight's duration. Old Nick: *"Done. He'll be right there when you're through. Whether you're glad of the trade by then — well. That's the one figure I never can predict."* |
| Decline the Companionship Clause. | No cost, no buff; Pip stays and fights at the Fool's side as ever. Old Nick: *"Keep the dog. Sentimental. I mean that as the compliment it isn't quite. Shall we, then?"* |

[All versions pick up here:]

Old Nick gathers the signed contracts — none, one, two, or all three — squares them, and
sets them precisely to one side. Then he stands, and rolls one shoulder, and the desk and
the pen and the green-leather ease of him do not vanish so much as *set aside*, revealing
underneath a thing that has been running a city on its charm for three hundred years and
has not had to actually fight for any of it in nearly as long.

**OLD NICK LOWRY**
> Terms concluded. Now the other half of the evening. Do try to make me *work* for it —
> it's been an age since anyone did, and a body forgets it's a pleasure.

## THE ENCOUNTER

### INT. THE UNDERVAULT: The Gilded Pit — GASLIGHT

*The arena is the pit's tiers: the marble floor, the ringed balconies climbing up and up,
the whole content city watching. Old Nick fights with chains — the same fine gold links
the residents wear — snapping them out to yank the Fool between tiers, to drag them back
off a ledge, to anchor them while he crosses the floor. The fight is built, per
`arcana.md` §XV, from three things: **chains, the pit's tiers, and the Fool's own
choices** — every clause the Fool signed is present on the floor, working exactly as it
was printed.*

**GAMEPLAY — parameterized by what the Fool signed:**

[If the Fool signed NO clauses — *brutal and proud*:]

Old Nick fights the whole fight himself, and it is the hardest single duel the Undervault
has to offer. No spectral second, no swollen Rose, no richer Fortune — only the Fool, Pip
at their side, and a contract-keeper who has decided to be, for once, entirely direct.
He is fast, and clean, and he does not gloat; there's no need. The chains come in
punishing rhythm across the tiers. When the Fool finally staggers him, Old Nick actually
*laughs* — the first ungoverned sound he's made all night.

**OLD NICK LOWRY** *(mid-fight, if no clauses signed)*
> You took *nothing*. Do you know how long it's been since someone took nothing? You
> walked into the one shop in the world that gives fair value and you paid full price in
> the only coin I don't stock — *pride*. I could kiss you. I could also end you. Both, I
> think. In that order.

[If the Fool signed SOME but not all clauses:]

Each signed clause manifests on the floor exactly as printed and no more: [if Coin: the
Fool's Fortune runs visibly richer, powers coming faster]; [if Flesh: the Rose blooms
overfull, one extra charge of mending in hand]; [if Companionship: a spectral second
stands at the Fool's shoulder, mirroring every Present-slot Trump the Fool casts, while
Pip's muffled bark carries from beyond the shut gate]. Old Nick fights straight — he does
not exercise the borrow-clause; that price was never signed. He notes the Fool's comforts

**OLD NICK LOWRY** *(mid-fight, if some clauses signed)*
> You took the *sensible* ones. Most do. A little comfort, a little edge, nothing you
> couldn't look at square in the morning. That's the trouble with sensible, though —
> [a chain snaps taut] — it leaves a man wondering what you'd have paid if I'd only asked
> nicer.

[If the Fool signed ALL THREE clauses — *he barely needs to fight*:]

The Fool enters richer, fuller-healed, flanked by a spectral echo of their own Present
Trump — and Old Nick, for the first minute, genuinely coasts, letting the buffs make the
fight look won, unhurried as a man watching a wager he already knows the result of. Then,
at the midpoint, he taps the stacked contracts on the desk, once, with two fingers, and
exercises the clause buried in the ordinary print of all three at once — a borrowing
right the Fool signed thrice without reading it three times.

**OLD NICK LOWRY** *(exercising the borrow-clause)*
> Now. You'll recall — you signed it, so you'll recall — that a fully executed set of my
> terms grants the house *reciprocal use of the signatory's instruments*. Small print.
> Large consequences. I did read it aloud. You were, I think, admiring the desk.

He reaches, without touching the Fool, and *lifts* the Fool's own Pocket Spread off them
— and turns each equipped Trump reversed, and casts them back:

- [the Fool's Past-slot Trump, reversed] — its passive turned against its owner, if the

- [the Fool's Present-slot Trump, reversed] — cast at the Fool with its corrupted effect,

- [the Fool's Future-slot Trump, reversed] — its fate-effect sprung against the Fool at

one of those cards, in ink, at the desk, and can go back and read where they said so.

**OLD NICK LOWRY** *(if all three, as the reversed Trumps land)*
> Everything you brought, turned the other way about. Everything you signed, spent on you.
> I never took a thing you didn't set down in front of me and *press a pen to*. That's not
> a betrayal, sweet Excuse. There's no such thing here. There's only the terms, and you
> read them, and you signed, and I am the one soul in all the Spread who does *precisely*
> what he said he would.

### INT. THE UNDERVAULT: The Falter — GASLIGHT

However the fight ran — earned brutal, bought easy, or somewhere between — it comes to the
same floor. The Fool lands the telling blow, and something goes out of Old Nick that has
nothing to do with the wound. He stops. He looks at his own hand as if the chain in it
were a thing he'd been handed by mistake. Beneath three centuries of charm and terms and
the smooth green-leather ease of him, a man who has not lost at anything in longer than he
can remember discovers that he is losing, and does not, at first, recognise the feeling.

**OLD NICK LOWRY** *(the falter)*
> That's — hm. That's not a term I recall drafting. Losing. I'd have priced it differently
> if I'd known the shape of it. [he sits, slowly, on the marble, without a desk to sit
> behind for the first time in an age] Do you know, I don't believe I ever wrote myself a
> clause for this. Careless. I'm never careless. What have I been *doing* down here.

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE — THE UNBINDING

The office cracks. Not the desk — the *office*: the ease, the smoothness, the whole gilded
apparatus of the contract-keeper comes apart at the seam like a seal breaking on a document
finally, at long last, allowed to close. And underneath it, surfacing the way a held breath
lets go, is a name he has heard shouted across this pit ten thousand times in three hundred
years and never once, not one single time, said of himself.

**OLD NICK LOWRY** *(barely)*
> They all call me it. Every day. "Old Nick," "Old Nick Lowry," across the floor, up the
> stair — I hear it more than any name's ever been heard. And I never... it was the
> office's name. The desk's name. Never *mine* to say.

He tests it. Slowly. Like a piece of cutlery he hasn't held in so long he's forgotten
which hand it goes in.

**NICK**
> ...Nick. *I'm* Nick. [a small, astonished, entirely un-awful laugh] Oh. Oh, that's — I'd
> forgotten there was a *me* under the terms of me. Three hundred years keeping everyone
> else's word. Nobody ever offered to keep mine.

He gets up off the marble a plainer man than the one who sat down on it — smaller, older
about the eyes, the charm gone quiet and the awfulness gone entirely, leaving only the
funny, which was always the part that was really his. He reaches into the air and draws a
single card, no flourish, and holds it out to the Fool with both hands, the way a man
closes a ledger he never expects to reopen.

**NICK**
> Trump the Fifteenth. Bargain, they'll call it — and they'll be right, it's the honest
> one, it always was. [If CONFESSED: You know what you're doing to all of us, region by
> region. You told me nothing and I read it off you at the desk in the first minute —
> you're the fellow spending down the whole world's chains, one signature at a time. I
> find I don't mind. It's the first deal in three centuries where I could see the whole
> price up front, and it's *fair*. That's rarer than you'd think. That's nearly holy.]
> Take it. Both hands — there. A thing given freely. I don't believe I've done one of
> those since before the desk. Turns out it's the only sort worth the ink.

**[Pocket Spread: Trump XV (Bargain) received. If the Companionship Clause was signed, the
gate above unlatches on its own the moment the office cracks, and Pip is already halfway
down the stair before the cutscene ends — the clause is void with its author.]**

END CUT SCENE

### INT. THE UNDERVAULT: The Chains Fall — GASLIGHT INTO FIRST LIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

It goes up through the pit like a change of pressure. On every tier, from every wrist and
ankle and belt-loop, the fine gold chains simply *open* — no drama, no snapping, just a
quiet universal unlatching, as if every contract in the Undervault reached its final
clause at once. Residents look down at their open chains. Some laugh. Some weep. Most,
after a moment, begin to climb — a slow, dazed, upward tide toward a stair most of them
haven't taken in decades.

[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND: at the top of the stair a real dawn is breaking, and the climbers
reach it blinking, hands raised against a genuine, moving light.]

[If not WS_SUN_UNBOUND: at the top of the stair waits the stalled, colourless not-quite-day
of the frozen world — no less strange to eyes that haven't seen any sky at all in years,
the climbers squinting up at a light that at least *is* one.]

## Open questions

- **The Flesh Clause petal cost — permanent or restorable?** Carried forward from the outline
  and still unresolved. `progression.md` should decide whether the surrendered max-Rose petal
  is a truly permanent reduction (a heavy price for one optional signature) or is healable at
  a later system (a Waystation shrine, a Star-region grace, a New Game+ restoration). The
  script prints it honestly as permanent to preserve the horror; if canon rules it restorable,
  the printed term must change in the same edit so Old Nick keeps never lying.
- **Naming tension (carried forward).** "Old Nick" is a real-world folk-epithet for the Devil
  and risks reading as an office-title rather than a private name — the Undervault already
  calls him "Old Nick Lowry" before this quest. This script resolves it exactly as the outline
  proposed: the epithet is the office's public shorthand, and the first-person "Nick" at the
  crack is the true return-of-name beat (he has never said it of himself). Flag still stands
  for a canon call: is a distinct, unheard-until-now given name warranted to make the beat
  land harder, or does "the office wore the name he could never claim" *itself* carry the
  theme? Recommend the latter — it is the sharpest expression of "offices eat people" in the
  game.
- **The three antechamber vignette residents (singer, mother) are unnamed ambient Minors.**
  The outline proposed promoting a named "gate resident" to `characters.md` before script;
  this script instead keeps the gate resident, the singer, and the mother unnamed (ambient,
  read by suit per `npc-system.md`), reserving the only named minor to canon's Merrow Slate.
  Confirm this is preferred, or promote one/more to `characters.md` if a recurring face is
  wanted for the freed-tiers aftermath.
- **Does declining all three clauses grant anything beyond Old Nick's respect (and the hardest
  fight)?** Outline recommended the respect *is* the reward; this script keeps that. Confirm
  no material acknowledgment (Renown, Almanack note) is wanted, per tone.
- **Borrow-clause scope with partial spreads.** The reversed-Trump clause fires only on
  all-three-signed and only borrows filled Pocket Spread slots. Confirm at combat tuning that
  a Fool arriving with a near-empty spread (legal, given order-independence) still gets a
  readable version of the beat — the script has Old Nick shrug off empty slots, but a spread
  with zero equipped Trumps would leave the clause with nothing to borrow; decide whether that
  edge needs a fallback (a generic reversed "house" cast) or is acceptable as a rare non-event.

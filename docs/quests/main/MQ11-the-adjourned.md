# MQ11 — The Adjourned

## Introduction

The player walks into the Assize's fog-grey courts to find the game's driest joke made
into a whole region: three hundred years of trials, every one adjourned, nothing ever
decided, and the accused waiting it out in patient, knitting queues. To reach the
Magistrate the Fool must first beat her bureaucracy — the correct docket, the correct
stamp, the correct queue — and the only thing that gets a Fool through it is being the
one card that fits no docket at all. Along the way the player nudges a small dispute
almost to a close (and learns why "almost" is the whole tragedy of the place), and drills
the fight's tempo in a mock-trial before ever meeting her. The Magistrate herself is
blindfolded, alone in the highest empty courtroom, and her scales store every blow the
Fool lands and pour the whole sum back on a loud, readable courtroom-bell rhythm — so the
fight is patience, not fury: build, stagger her before the bell, dump the scales, repeat.
She also, quietly, audits how the player has fought the entire game, and cheap habits cost
double. Unbinding her renders the first verdict in three centuries — and empties the
prisons, three genuinely bad actors included, each walking free to trouble the Spread
later. `requires` is empty: MQ11 may be played in any order, so its scenes carry
`[If WS_…]` and `[If CONFESSED]` variants throughout.

## QUEST: THE ADJOURNED

### EXT. THE ASSIZE: The Court Approach — PERPETUAL GREY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The road out of the fog resolves, slowly, into architecture: court complexes stacked on
court complexes, all the same wet grey stone, all under the same flat foglight that has
not once, in three hundred years, resolved into either a morning or an evening. Nothing
casts a shadow. Everything is exactly as legible as a form.

The Fool follows a colonnade past the first of the queues. It is a very long queue. It is
also entirely at ease: the accused of three centuries sit on folding stools and cushions
worn to their shapes, and every last one of them is *knitting* — scarves, mostly, of a
length that has stopped being clothing and started being landscape. Nobody is impatient.
Nobody is anywhere near the front. The front is not visibly closer than it was a hundred
years ago.

### INT. THE ASSIZE: The Docketing Hall — GREY

CUT SCENE

A cathedral-sized hall given over entirely to pigeonholes: a wall of them, floor to
vanishing ceiling, each stuffed with rolled and ribboned paper, every roll a case that
was opened and never closed. At a single brass wicket beneath it all stands CLERK
PETTIBONE — narrow, immaculate, a man built for a collar — with a ledger open before him

**CLERK PETTIBONE**
> Name, docket, and matter, if you please. If you've no docket, the docket queue is the
> third colonnade, second cloister, first stool. If you've no *matter*, I'm afraid I
> can't in good conscience issue you a docket, and you'll want the Enquiries queue, which
> is — [he consults nothing, knows it cold] — closed, pending a hearing, since the Stall.
> [a small, precise cough] You see the difficulty.

**THE FOOL**
> I've come to see the Magistrate.

Pettibone's pen stops. Not alarmed — *interested*, the way a man is interested in the one

**CLERK PETTIBONE**
> To *see* her. Yes. People do ask. Now — under whose docket, in what matter, and holding
> which place in which queue? Because I have kept every queue in this Assize in perfect
> order for three hundred years, young — [he squints] — young *whatever-you-are, and I
> have never once lost a file, and I do not intend to begin with you.

### INT. THE ASSIZE: The Mock Court — GREY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

A drilling-room dressed as a courtroom: a dock, a bench, and above the bench a modest set
of brass scales on a chain, plainly a training rig. A single BAILIFF BLANK stands in the
dock — a Blank bearing a court-rank tabard — and above *it* hangs a second small scale, a
miniature of the Magistrate's own.

**[Tutorial prompt: this fight rehearses the boss tempo. Read the three beats below and
practice them; the mock court will not open the High Stair until the Fool clears it
without eating a full pour.]**

The Bailiff Blank's rig teaches the whole fight in three moves:

- **Build.** Every strike the Fool lands does *not* register as damage — it drops into the
  Blank's little scale, which visibly tips lower and begins to glow.
- **The bell.** A practice bell — a soft one — swings back on a long, obvious wind-up. On
  the ring, the scale pours: the stored total fires back as one fat, dodgeable riposte.
- **Stagger.** Land a stagger *before* the bell and the scale dumps harmlessly, spilling
  its glow across the floor. The cycle resets. That is the entire secret, rehearsed here
  where it costs nothing.

**THE QUERENT** *(over the drill)*
> They rehearse verdicts down here they're never allowed to hand down. Three centuries of
> dress rehearsal for a play that never opens. Do pay attention, though — the dress
> rehearsal is the only warning you get, and she does not do a second one.

The Bailiff Blank staggers on the final cycle, its little scale dumping for good; the card
peels off its tabard and drifts up the stairwell it has guarded, and the gate to the High
Stair grinds open behind it for the first time in a very long time.

### INT. THE ASSIZE: The Highest Courtroom — GREY

CUT SCENE

The room is vast and completely empty — no gallery, no clerk, no dock, no accused. Only a
bench at the far end, and above it, hung from the dark, an enormous set of scales, both
pans dead level, motionless. And at the bench: THE MAGISTRATE. Robed. Still. Blindfolded
— a plain grey band across the eyes — hands folded, listening to a room with nothing in
it to hear.

She does not turn her head. She has known the Fool was coming since the docket that was no
docket.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> A petitioner who is no one, in a matter that is nothing, holding a place in the one
> queue that was never meant to move. [a dry, precise beat] The filing is, I'll grant,
> flawless. Clerk Pettibone does excellent work. He always has. It has never once helped.

**THE FOOL**
> I've come for a verdict.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> Everyone comes for a verdict. It is the single thing this court exists to give and the
> single thing it cannot. Do you know what fairness is, with the judging taken out of it?
> [she lifts the level scales a fraction; they settle back level] It is *this.* Weighing
> forever. Deciding never. Scrupulous to the last, and utterly, perfectly useless.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> [If CONFESSED: I know what you are, and I know what follows you — an ending, region by
> region, for everyone. So you'll understand the irony, being precise yourself: I am
> about to render the last verdict I shall ever be afraid to render. After you, there are
> no more cases. Only the sentence. Let us be exact about it while I still can.]

She rises. The great scales above her come alive — both pans beginning, faintly, to
glow — and tip in time with her, hungry, level, waiting to be fed.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> The charge is simply how you have conducted yourself, all this long way here. I make no
> accusation. I only weigh. Everything you deal me, these scales will hold — and on the
> bell, return to you in full. Strike as you please. The court is, as ever, *listening.*

END CUT SCENE

### INT. THE ASSIZE: The Highest Courtroom — THE FIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[Combat per `arcana.md` §XI — real-time, like every fight. The loop, exactly as
rehearsed in the mock court:]**

- **The scales fill.** Every point of damage the Fool deals the Magistrate is *stored* in
  the hanging scales rather than felling her — the loaded pan sinks and brightens as it
  climbs toward full.
- **The bell.** On a loud, unmistakable, well-telegraphed courtroom-bell wind-up, she
  pours: the entire stored total fires back as one single riposte, to be dodged clean
  (Fool's Chance rewards a perfect read) or suffered whole. This is a boss pattern, not a
  turn — the room keeps moving.
- **Stagger to dump.** Land a stagger *before* the bell rings and the scales dump
  harmlessly, spilling their light across the floor; the cycle resets and the Fool builds
  again. Tempo, not fury: the player who swings wildest feeds the biggest pour back into
  their own face.
- **Cheap tactics weigh double.** Blows struck from behind, healing the Rose mid-duel, and
  reversed-Trump burdens shrugged off onto her all register in the scales at **double**
  weight — the fight is quietly auditing the whole game's habits, and the loaded pour is
  the receipt.

**THE MAGISTRATE Random Lines** *(mid-fight; pick per rhythm)*

> Build all you like. It only means the bill runs longer. Everything comes back. That is
> the one law I have never been prevented from enforcing.

> There is the bell. You *heard* it coming. Everyone always hears it coming. Whether you
> did anything about it — that is the whole of the case.

> Staggered, before the ring. Good. That is not mercy. That is simply timing. I have a
> great deal of respect for timing. It is the only thing three hundred years leaves a
> person.

**THE MAGISTRATE — the audit** *(conditional barks — she cites the player's own habits
back at them in general, systemic terms; no personal cruelty, only the weighing)*

> [If the Fool has favored blows struck from behind: The petitioner favors the strike the
> struck party cannot see. Noted. The scales note it twice. They note *everything* — that
> is rather the trouble with scales.]

> [If the Fool has leaned on the Rose mid-fight through the game: The petitioner mends
> mid-argument and calls it fair. I do not fault it. I only weigh it. Double, as it
> happens. Balance is not sentimental.]

> [If the Fool has made a habit of dodging reversed-Trump burdens onto foes: The
> petitioner passes their sentence to whoever stands nearest. An old trick. An honest
> court has seen it ten thousand times. Here it comes back with interest.]

> [If the Fool has fought clean and patient all game: The petitioner fights as though
> someone were always watching. [a beat — almost warm] Someone always was. It is a
> lighter pour, for that. Not nothing. But lighter.]

At full weight, on the last dumped pour, the Magistrate staggers and does not rise into
her stance again. The great scales swing wide, spill their whole stored light down the
walls — and, for the first time in three centuries, come to rest *unlevel.* One pan lower
than the other. A decision, made at last, in brass.

### INT. THE ASSIZE: The Highest Courtroom — THE VERDICT

CUT SCENE

The Magistrate stands in the settling light, blindfold still on, head tilted at the
scales she cannot see but can, unmistakably, *feel* — because they are no longer level,
and they have not been anything but level for longer than she has had a name.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> They've... tipped. The scales have *tipped.* [her voice does something it has not done
> in three hundred years — it moves] That is a verdict. That is an actual — after all
> this, a decision, rendered, *done*—

The office cracks. Not her body: the *robe*, the bench, the whole grey apparatus of the
role, fracturing along a hundred seams like ice going off a pond in spring. The blindfold
is the last thing to go — the grey band loosening, slipping, falling away — and under it
are ordinary eyes, blinking, wet, seeing a room for the first time.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> Oh. *Oh.* I can — there's a room. There was always a room, and I never once—

Something surfaces behind the eyes, the way a name does, the way the ladder-dispute so
badly wanted to and could not: a decision finally allowed to close.

**THE MAGISTRATE**
> ...*Prudence.*

She says it carefully, weighing it, the way she has weighed everything — and finds, for
once, that it balances.

**PRUDENCE**
> Prudence. Yes. That was the name before the office. Cause, and effect. Weigh, and then —
> *and then decide.* I only ever had the first half. Three hundred years of the first
> half.

clear, dry, level regard — a fairness that, for the first time, has somewhere to land.

**PRUDENCE**
> [If CONFESSED: You knew what a verdict from you would cost. You came for it precisely.

And out of the fog, past the Fool, comes a single petitioner at a *run* — an old man,
clutching a slip of paper, verdict in hand, his three-hundred-year-old case decided and
*done* — and he is not walking, and he is not knitting, and he is not holding anyone's
place. He is skipping. Actually skipping, badly, joints unused to it, laughing at the
sheer novelty of having somewhere he is finally allowed to *go.* He does not stop. He does
not explain. He is out of the gate and gone into the grey the wrong way — the way *out* —
before the Fool can say a word.

**THE QUERENT**
> There. That one. Three hundred years he stood in a line, and today he got an answer and
> a pair of legs to run off on. That's the day's one unmixed joy, and I'd not have you
> miss it for all Pettibone's grief — both things are true at once, up here. That's rather
> the Assize's whole trick: a wall of unfinished paper, and then a man skips out the door.
> Come on. Other cards still won't turn themselves.

The eastern road out of the Assize, closed behind three centuries of adjournment, stands
open — and this time, when a case ends, it stays ended. MQ11 concludes here.

## Open questions

- **The audit mechanism (technical design).** The Magistrate cites the player's real
  habits (backstab preference, mid-fight Rose healing, reversed-burden dodges) back at
  them and weighs those tactics double. This is written as *conditional bark variants and
  a scoring rule*, deliberately **without** inventing any `WS_*` or persistent tracking
  flag. How the fight actually reads long-run play habits — a lightweight rolling combat
  telemetry read at fight-load, a per-session tally, or a simpler in-fight "this encounter
  only" measure — is a technical-design decision needed before implementation. Whatever it
  measures, it must never be surfaced to the player as a number.
- **The concrete "cheap tactics" list.** The exact set of tactics that weigh double
  against the scales (and their double-weight multiplier) needs a firm definition from
  `combat.md` before the fight can be tuned; the script names backstabs, mid-duel Rose
  healing, and reversed-Trump burdens dodged onto her as the canonical three, per
  `arcana.md` §XI, but the boundaries (e.g. does a Fool's Chance counter count as a
  backstab? does a Trump-granted heal count as "mid-duel healing"?) are unresolved.
- **On-screen freed prisoners (carried forward, now recommended-resolved).** The three
  guilty are staged in the aftermath as **wordless, unsettling glimpses** with no dialogue
  and no pursuit, each explicitly handed to its side quest — the recommended handling from
  the outline. Confirm this satisfies the side quests' need to own their own reveals, or
  decide whether any of the three should be cut from MQ11 entirely and introduced fresh in
  their own quests.
- **The minor disputants.** The ladder-dispute petitioners are written as unnamed ambient
  types (First/Second Petitioner) to avoid inventing canon. If the Arbitrate dispute-event
  system later wants a named recurring test-case dispute, one of these may need promoting
  to a named NPC in `characters.md` — flagged, not decided.

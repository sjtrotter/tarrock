# MQ04 — Set in Stone

## Introduction

The player comes to the Bastion, a granite city that runs like clockwork because it is
terrified of what happens if it doesn't — gong-timed streets, papers checked at every
threshold, citizens who live to the minute and have not missed a schedule in three
hundred years. At the city's centre, unmoved since the Stall, sits a forty-foot colossus
of law-graven stone: the Emperor, who has not risen from his cube throne in three
centuries because his own law forbids amendment, including of himself. Reaching him is
bureaucracy before it is combat. No one approaches the plinth without a Writ of Audience,
issued only through proper channels, and the player meets Clerk Anselm — correct on the
surface, quietly exhausted underneath — who processes it. The player may earn the writ
honestly through Anselm's stamp-chain or skip the paperwork and slip past the curfew
checkpoint without it; the choice changes Anselm's last line and nothing else. Then the
fight, which is a *climb*: the Fool scales the Emperor while he attacks on a strict,
learnable rota — a timetable to be mastered, not survived — until three of his four
carved Edicts are broken and he does the single most frightening thing in the fight. He
amends his own schedule. Once. Unbinding him grants Trump IV (Decree), throws the gates
open, and lets petty crime back into the world for the first time in three centuries.
If the player reaches the Bastion after MQ13 (Death, "An Ending"), the key scenes carry
`[If CONFESSED: …]` variants — the amendment lands as resignation rather than defiance,
and Anselm's grief for his tidy ledgers turns a shade darker.

### EXT. THE BASTION: The Outer Gate — GONG-HOUR

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The road ends at a wall of dressed granite so precise the joints look drawn rather than
laid, grey shot through with thin lines of exact gold. A gate the height of six men
stands open to the width of exactly one cart. Above it, a great bronze gong hangs in an
iron cradle, and as the Fool approaches it is struck — once, enormous, felt in the
teeth — and the whole city moves at the sound: shutters open in unison down every street
beyond, a market's awnings unroll as one, a line of citizens steps off a kerb together
like a single body. Then the gong-note fades and everything is still again, waiting for
the next hour to tell it what to do.

### INT. THE BASTION: The Registry Hall — GONG-HOUR

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

A hall the size of a cathedral, given over entirely to lines. Rope-railed queues fold

gong that hangs from the ceiling and marks the seconds the way the great gong marks the
hours.

goodwife with a petition worn soft as cloth, holding her place with the serenity of
someone who long ago stopped expecting to reach the front. She nods to the Fool, kindly,
and does not give up her spot.

### EXT. THE BASTION: The Plinth — DUSK-GONG

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The empty square. Up close, the colossus is not a statue of the Emperor — it *is* the
Emperor, granite gone to living stone or living stone gone to granite, no seam between
throne and man. Four Edicts are carved into him in letters a foot deep: one banded about
each wrist, one yoked across both shoulders, one crowning his brow — the founding law of
the Bastion, cut into the lawgiver himself, unbroken since the Stall. His eyes are closed.
His hands rest on his knees. He has not moved in three hundred years, and the city has
mistaken this for peace.

As the Fool crosses the empty ground toward the plinth's base, the great head turns —
the first movement it has made in three centuries — grinding, slow, a sound like a
quarry waking.

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE

**THE EMPEROR**
> Unscheduled. Unwrit. Unheralded. *(a pause the size of the square)* Or writ, and late.
> It makes no difference to the seat. The seat does not receive petitioners. The seat
> has one office, and the office is to hold. To hold is the whole of the law, and the law
> is the whole of the seat.

**THE FOOL**
> I've come to unbind you.

**THE EMPEROR**
> That word has no filing. There is no Edict permitting release, and what is not
> permitted does not occur. You are, at most, an irregularity. A clerical fault, come to
> the plinth on legs. Faults are corrected. Stand still and be corrected.

He does not say his name. He does not say *I* and a name in one breath; there is no *I*
here to attach a name to, only the seat, only the office, only the holding. He rises —
the first rising in three hundred years — granite shearing dust from his shoulders, and
the four Edicts brighten along their carved depths like law remembering it can bite.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. THE BASTION: The Colossus — DUSK-GONG (the climb-fight)

**[Gameplay — the climb-fight (arcana.md §IV, encounter tier L): the Fool climbs the

**THE EMPEROR** *(mid-climb, each line a decree rather than a taunt)*
> The first hold is unlawful. *(sweep)* The second hold is unlawful. *(grip)* You will

**THE EMPEROR**
> One Edict spent. Note that the seat does not adjust. The seat does not learn. To learn
> is to change, and the law forbids the seat to change. You may rely on this. Everyone

### CUT SCENE

The Fool starts the last climb toward the crown on the count that has worked eleven times.
The colossus does not move on the count. The great gong strikes — and the Emperor holds
*through* it, one full beat, two, breaking his own rhythm for the first time in three
hundred years. The dust freezes on his shoulders. Somewhere below, the whole city's
second-gong stutters, and a thousand citizens miss a step in unison, because the thing
that keeps their time has just, for one impossible instant, kept none.

**THE EMPEROR** *(and for the first time the voice is not level)*
> ...The seat amends the schedule.

He says it the way a man confesses a murder — because it is one. The one law under all
the others, the law that forbids all amendment including its own, and the seat has just
broken it, spent it, to stop a single fault on its arm. If *this* can change, nothing in
the Bastion was ever safe; the floor of the world has just been shown to be a lid.

### EXT. THE BASTION: The Opened Gates — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Over the following in-game hours the Bastion comes unstrung, gently. The great gong is
struck at the hour and, for the first time, nobody moves in unison to it — a shutter opens
early here, a market awning stays furled there, a queue simply disperses because someone
in it thought of somewhere better to be. The gates that admitted one cart's width stand
thrown fully open. The curfew barrier is gone; the guards on the plinth terrace stand
about with their halberds shouldered, plainly unsure what a guard is *for* when there is
no schedule to enforce.

[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND: the gongs are silent now entirely — the city has finally let the sun
keep the time, and the hours arrive soft and uneven and real.]

At a granite-fronted market stall, the Bastion's first petty theft in three hundred years
is in slow, catastrophic progress: an apple lifted by a fumbling hand, its owner not yet
sure what he's witnessing, two guards converging with the awkward hunger of men handed a
task they've forgotten the shape of. Nobody quite knows the steps. Everyone looks, if
anything, relieved to have an ordinary wrong to chase.

**[Barks below; the Fool may proceed to the Registry to close with Anselm.]**

### INT. THE BASTION: The Registry Hall — DAY (closing)

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The Registry is half-empty. Queues that stood three centuries have dissolved; rope-rails
hang slack. At his tall window, Clerk Anselm sits before a single sheet — the last writ
of the old order, the record of an audience that ended the thing the audience was *for* —
and he does not stamp it, because for the first time in his professional life he cannot
work out what it *is*.

**CLERK ANSELM**

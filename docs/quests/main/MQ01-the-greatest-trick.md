# MQ01 — The Greatest Trick

## Introduction

The player arrives at the Prestige straight off the Cliff's landing, met first by Flick,
the carnival's barker, before the main tent's three-act gate sends them through the
House of Mirrors, the Menagerie, and the High Wire to earn three ticket stubs. Along the
way the player meets the Troupe — Old Tomkin, Marigold Fen, Perrin Loom, Tansy Quill —
and hears each one's opinion on what should happen to the show, should it ever end. The
main event is the Bataleur, the Magician bound to three centuries of the same perfect
trick; unbinding him is this quest's first Trump and the first time the Pocket Spread's
Present slot opens (`progression.md` §Slot unlock pacing). The quest closes on a choice —
send the freed Troupe travelling or let them settle — and both options are written to
ache. If the player reaches the Prestige after MQ13 (Death, "An Ending"), every scene
below carries a `[If CONFESSED: …]` variant: the Prestige already knows, by then, what a
visit from the Fool means.

## QUEST: THE GREATEST TRICK

### EXT. THE PRESTIGE: The Crossroads Gate — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Striped canvas rises ahead like a fairground cathedral, gaslit and warm against a sky
gone the colour of a bruise. Banners that should have faded decades ago snap crisp in a
wind that never quite stops. Popcorn smell, old and sweet and a little strange, hangs
over everything. A hand-lettered arch reads WELCOME, WELCOME, ALWAYS WELCOME, the paint
retouched so many times the letters have gone soft at the edges.

FLICK, a lanky young man in a striped waistcoat two sizes too enthusiastic for him,
spots the Fool climbing out of the haywain and abandons his post at a rope line to
sprint over.

**FLICK Random Lines** *(repeatable greeting; pick one at random)*

> Fresh face! Oh, we do love a fresh face round here — sit anywhere, the good seats
> are all the seats!

> You fell out of the *sky*, friend, and I still haven't decided if that's the best
> ticket I've ever sold or the worst.

> [If CONFESSED: Well now. Word travels even inside a tent that never lets anyone out.
> You're the one turning the cards loose. Best seat in the house, then — on me.]

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE

**FLICK**
> Flick, Page of Wands, and personally, *personally*, the first friendly face you'll
> meet on the whole Spread — I checked. Welcome to the Prestige! Show's been running
> three hundred years and it has never, not once, had a night this good.

**THE FOOL**
> Every night's the best night?

**FLICK**
> Every single one! That's showbusiness for you.

END CUT SCENE

### INT. THE PRESTIGE: The Grand Tent, Audience Rows — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Passing the main tent on the way to the side-shows, the Fool crosses rows and rows of
audience benches, packed shoulder to shoulder with folk in three-hundred-year-old
Sunday best, eyes front, waiting for a show that has, technically, never stopped. Nobody
turns to look at the Fool. Nobody has turned to look at anything else in a very long
time.

In the third row, feet not quite reaching the ground, sits WREN — a girl of perhaps
eight, ribbon in her hair, utterly unaged, swinging her legs to a rhythm only she can
hear.

**WREN**
> Are you new? We don't get new very often. Actually we don't get new *ever*. You're
> the newest thing in three hundred years, I should think, other than the popcorn, and
> even that's mostly the same popcorn.

**THE FOOL**
> How long have you been sitting here?

**WREN**
> Since before my mum was born, I think. Isn't that funny.

She says it exactly like it's funny. It is not, quite, and the tent seems to know it —
a beat of held quiet before Wren goes back to swinging her feet and watching the empty
main stage, patient as stone.

### INT. THE PRESTIGE: The House of Mirrors — NIGHT

GAMEPLAY: A canvas maze of angled looking-glass, the Fool's own reflection multiplying
and sliding out of step at every turn; the tent's single genuine act, TANSY QUILL,
stands at the centre in a coat of sewn mirror-shards, and the "puzzle" is simply
watching closely enough to catch the one reflection that breathes, blinks, and casts a
shadow the glass ones don't — following it correctly through three chambers earns the
stub, and the observation habit it teaches (watch for the one detail that doesn't
match) is exactly the tell the Fool will need in the main tent.

**TANSY QUILL**
> Everybody thinks the trick's in the glass. It's never in the glass. It's in whichever
> one of us is fool enough to look real while we're doing it.

**THE FOOL**
> How do I tell which one's you?

**TANSY QUILL**
> Watch for the one who's tired. Glass doesn't get tired. People do — even people
> who've been doing this same trick for three hundred years. *Especially* those people.

**TANSY QUILL Random Lines**

> If the show ever stops, I go where nobody's looked at the same trick twice. Fresh
> eyes are the only currency that matters to me.

> [If CONFESSED: You'll unbind him tonight, won't you. Good. He's been tired in that
> coat a lot longer than I've been tired in mine.]

The Fool finds the real Tansy Quill among her reflections; she stamps the first stub
herself, pleased.

### INT./EXT. THE PRESTIGE: The Menagerie Tent — NIGHT

GAMEPLAY: A wagon-yard of paper beasts — folded, hinged, animated by some carnival
trick of wind and wire, lions and elephants and a very large paper peacock, all
lifelike enough to snap and startle but harmless once handled right. PERRIN LOOM, the
Troupe's strongman, is up to his elbows resetting a paper elephant's leg-hinge and
waves the Fool over without looking up.

**[Tutorial prompt: call Pip's Harry command — send Pip to harry/herd a target.]**

**PERRIN LOOM**
> Wind gets in the paper, they go a bit wild, nothing worse. Send the dog at the
> stray ones — he'll herd better than I ever could, and I lift *bridges*.

The Fool sets Pip loose on three loose paper beasts that have wandered from their
pens; Pip herds each back into its wagon with brisk, businesslike delight.

**PERRIN LOOM**
> There. Good dog. Good — whatever you are, too.

**PERRIN LOOM Random Lines**

> Whatever the others decide, travelling or settling, I'll go where they go. Not much
> point being strong on your own.

> [If CONFESSED: I've carried this whole show on my back a long while. Wouldn't mind
> setting it down, if it comes to that. Wouldn't mind at all.]

Perrin stamps the second stub into the Fool's program with a hand the size of a dinner
plate.

### EXT. THE PRESTIGE: The High Wire — NIGHT

GAMEPLAY: A wire strung absurdly high between two poles above the carnival green,
lantern-lit, no net (a net is offered, comically, and visibly never once needed) — the
Fool crosses it in three lengthening stretches, wind picking up each time, a pure nerve
-and-balance traversal test with no combat at all. OLD TOMKIN waits at the far pole,
juggling five clubs he has, by his own count, dropped exactly zero times in three
hundred years; MARIGOLD FEN sits below at a card table, reading fortunes for anyone
brave enough to walk.

**MARIGOLD FEN** *(before the crossing)*
> Shall I read your cards before you risk your neck? ...No, don't answer, I already
> know. I always already know. It's the least funny thing about this job.

**[Tutorial prompt: cross the wire — traversal only, timed balance input.]**

At the far side, Old Tomkin catches all five clubs in one hand without appearing to
notice he's finished.

**OLD TOMKIN**
> Three hundred years, five clubs, not one dropped. Some nights I think about letting
> just the one go, just to see. Never quite manage it. Suppose that's its own kind of
> trick.

**OLD TOMKIN Random Lines**

> If the tent ever folds for good, I'd not mind new roads under it. Same act, new
> faces watching. Might even drop a club for them.

> [If CONFESSED: Go on then. Turn the last card loose on us. I've been ready to drop
> something for longer than you've been alive.]

**MARIGOLD FEN Random Lines**

> I read cards for a living in a world made of them. No, I've never once found that
> funny. Ask me again in three hundred years.

> If it comes to it, I'd rather we settled somewhere. A woman likes to lay her cards
> on the *same* table twice in a while.

Old Tomkin stamps the third and final stub.

|---|---|
| Will you read mine? | I already have, dear. You're the sort of card that doesn't sit still for a reading. Comes with the title, I expect. |
| What do you see for the show? | An ending, eventually. A good one, if I'm reading it right — and I am always reading it right. |
| I'd rather not know. *(earnest)* | Wisest answer anyone's given me all century. Off you go. |

[All versions pick up here:]

Three stubs stamped. The main tent's flap glows gold ahead, and the crowd noise from
inside swells like something has, at long last, actually been waiting for the Fool
specifically.

### INT. THE PRESTIGE: The Main Tent — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[Tutorial prompt: present all three stubs at the main tent gate to enter.]**

The ring inside is vast, gilt and red velvet gone soft with centuries of the same
performance. Every seat is full. Every eye, for the first time all night, is on the
Fool.

CUT SCENE

Spotlights swing down. THE BATALEUR steps into the ring — top hat, tails, a showman's
smile worn thin as the ring's own velvet — and spreads his arms to the crowd like a
man who has made this exact gesture ten thousand times because he has.

**THE BATALEUR**
> Ladies! Gentlemen! And whatever climbed out of that haywain earlier tonight — a
> volunteer! Our first new volunteer in three hundred years!

The crowd — Wren included, several rows back — gasps in perfect, well-rehearsed unison.

**THE BATALEUR**
> [If CONFESSED: Oh, don't look so grave about it. Everyone in these seats knows
> exactly what a volunteer becomes, this late in the show. Doesn't stop the trick
> being a good one.]
>
> Now then. Watch closely. This is the greatest trick this ring has ever held, and it
> is about to be performed on *you*.

END CUT SCENE

GAMEPLAY: Three spotlights snap on around the ring, each catching an identical Bataleur
— top hat, tails, smile — so that three of him circle the Fool at once. Only one is
real; the tell, per the House of Mirrors' lesson, is small and physical, not magical —
the real Bataleur's buttonhole rose wilts a fraction more with every exchange, the
counterfeits' staying waxwork-perfect. Landing hits on the correct double staggers the
real Bataleur; landing them on a decoy bursts it into confetti — from which a
stagehand Blank straightens up, brushing itself off, and rejoins the fight as a lesser
enemy.

**THE BATALEUR** *(mid-fight patter, over the illusions)*
> Which one, which one! A showman never tells his own secrets — good policy, that,
> keeps a man employed for three centuries running!

At half health the ring itself flips — the whole stage rotating to dump Fool and
Bataleur alike into the under-stage: ropes, counterweights, trapdoor mechanisms, every
piece of the illusion exposed in unglamorous timber and rust. The duplicates fight on
here as stagehand Blanks, cruder now, their strings visible.

**THE BATALEUR**
> As above, so below! Every good trick's got machinery holding it up — mine's just
> been holding the *same* trick up a touch longer than most.

The Fool lands the final blow against the real Bataleur amid the under-stage's
counterweights. He staggers, sits heavily on an overturned prop crate, and the office
around him — the top hat, the patter, three hundred years of the same smile — audibly,
visibly cracks, like a plaster mask coming apart at the seam.

### CUT SCENE

**THE BATALEUR**
> That's — that's rather the finish, isn't it. Funny. I don't believe I ever actually
> reached the finish before—

He stops. Something moves under the crack in the office — not injury, a name,
surfacing the way a held breath finally lets go.

**THE BATALEUR**
> ...*Wicke.*

He says it slowly, tasting the shape of it, the way a man just off a desert road
tastes water and has to stop a moment to believe it's real.

**WICKE**
> Wicke. That's — yes. That's me. I'd almost forgotten the *sound* of it.

He gets up off the crate a different man than sat down on it — looser, older around
the eyes, entirely unperformed. He looks at the Fool properly, showman to showman, for
the first time all night.

**WICKE**
> [If CONFESSED: You already know what this is, don't you. What I am, what you're
> doing to every last one of us, region by region. I find I don't mind, this close up.
> Might be the first honest audience I've had in three hundred years.]
>
> First new volunteer in three centuries, and you turn out to be the one act I never
> saw coming. That's — that's rather good, actually. That's a *good* trick.

He draws a card from thin air — no flourish this time, no patter, just a man handing
something over because it's finally, simply, his to give.

**WICKE**
> Trump I. Manifest, they'll call it, if anyone ever writes it down properly. Take it
> — hands were always the trick, you know. Mine, yours. Everybody's, really.

**[Tutorial prompt: the Pocket Spread's Present slot unlocks. Slot Manifest to try its
Present effect — conjure a hand, grab and throw.]**

END CUT SCENE

### INT. THE PRESTIGE: The Grand Tent, Audience Rows — NIGHT

CUT SCENE

The audience rises. Slowly, all at once, three hundred years of sitting coming undone
in a single ragged wave — knees cracking audibly up and down the rows, breaths held
since some forgotten punchline finally, finally let go. Somewhere in the back, an old
man finishes a laugh he plainly started a very long time ago, the sound arriving in two
separate centuries.

Wren stands on her bench to see better, tugging her mother's sleeve.

**WREN**
> What happens now? What does everybody do now?

Wicke looks out over the rising crowd — the first time in three hundred years he's had
an honest answer for any of them.

**WICKE**
> Anything. Anything at all, poppet. That's rather the whole idea.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. THE PRESTIGE: Backstage, the Bataleur's Office — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Backstage, past the ropes and the crated confetti, an old office door — long jammed,
now hanging loose on a cracked hinge — opens onto a cramped room of unopened mail,
contracts never signed, and a single chair that has clearly not been sat in for three
centuries. Wicke sits in it like it still fits. The Troupe gathers loosely in the
doorway: Old Tomkin still holding his clubs, Marigold Fen with her cards, Perrin Loom
ducking the doorframe, Tansy Quill still faintly mirror-bright.

An old audience regular — FERRIDGE, front-row for three hundred years, program still
clutched in one hand — lingers at the edge of the group, not quite able to leave.

**FERRIDGE**
> I don't rightly know what I'm meant to do with an evening that hasn't got a show in
> it. Silly thing to grieve, I know. Grieving it all the same.

### EXT. THE PRESTIGE: The Crossroads — DAYS LATER

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Over the following in-game days, the carnival visibly comes down around the player:
canvas struck and folded, gaslamps dismantled and crated, the WELCOME arch's soft-worn
letters finally taken down and stacked. `[If WS_TROUPE_TRAVELING: wagons line up at
the crossroads, wheels greased, Old Tomkin already juggling to pass the time before
departure.]` `[If WS_TROUPE_SETTLED: the same canvas gets repurposed into awnings and
market stalls, the green re-staked as a proper town square.]` Flick, whichever way it
goes, is still exactly as delighted as he was on page one.

**FLICK Random Lines**

> Best show we ever ran, and the very last one! Bit of a mixed bag, feelings-wise, if
> I'm honest, which I try never to be in this line of work.

> [If WS_TROUPE_TRAVELING: Road's calling, apparently. Never been much further than
> the crossroads myself. Exciting, that.]

> [If WS_TROUPE_SETTLED: Turns out I'm rather good at running a market stall. Who
> knew. Not me, twelve hours ago.]

The eastern road beyond the Prestige, long closed behind the show's endless run, lies
open for the first time in three hundred years.

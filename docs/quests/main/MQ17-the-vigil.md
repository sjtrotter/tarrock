# MQ17 — The Vigil

## Introduction

The player arrives at the Mere, a night-locked lakeland under one enormous fixed star,
and finds — for the first and only time — a region that will not be fought through.
Fortune does not charge here, Blanks never enter, and the Warden of the Mere does not
ask to be defeated. She asks the Fool to keep one night's vigil beside her: tend the
shoreline's wish-lights, hear out three pilgrims who come to the water in the dark, and
sit a long while with Pip on the jetty. There is no timer but the vigil's own unhurried
pace, no puzzle to solve, no enemy to read. At the vigil's end she gives the Trump
freely — the only Arcanum in the game unbound by kindness alone. This is the quietest
quest in Tarrock, set down on purpose among its hardest neighbours; it should feel like
the whole game exhaling. The player *can* raise the Bindle to her anyway. She will not
defend herself, the Trump comes all the same, and nothing punishes the choice — but the
Mere goes permanently dimmer afterward, one light out and one voice fewer at the water,
and no one ever mentions it. Card 17 arrives well into the journey: every scene below
carries an `[If CONFESSED]` variant, and the Star's return is half of the game's
Sun-and-Star order motif, so the aftermath is written both ways (`[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND]`
/ `[If NOT WS_SUN_UNBOUND]`).

## QUEST: THE VIGIL

### EXT. THE MERE: The Shoreline Road — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The road comes down out of low hills and simply stops being a road, becoming instead a
long spit of pale shingle at the edge of black, unmoving water. There is no sun to have
set; the Mere has always been night, and the night is enormous and calm. One star hangs
over the middle of the lake — too large, too near, too bright to be any real star — and
its twin lies perfectly still on the water beneath it, so that the whole world seems
folded along the line where the lake meets the dark.

**[No combat prompt appears. If the player opens the Pocket Spread here, the Fortune
meter is greyed with a small note: *the Mere does not charge*. No Blanks spawn anywhere
in the region.]**

Along the shingle, a row of wish-lanterns on iron stems stands unlit and waiting, each
one leaning slightly toward the water like reeds. Wooden jetties reach out over the
Mere at intervals, and at the mouths of little inlets sit the dark stone rings of
wish-wells, dry and quiet. Nothing here moves quickly. Nothing here moves much at all.

Pip does not range ahead the way he does everywhere else. He walks close at the Fool's
heel, ears soft, and when the water comes fully into view he lets out a long breath
through his nose and slows to a saunter, as though he has finally reached the one place
on the whole Spread he was walking toward.

**THE QUERENT** *(quieter than the player has ever heard them)*
> Ah. Here. I'll not talk over this one much, little Excuse — some nights don't want a
> voice-over, and this is the only night in three hundred years that's kept its own
> counsel. Go down to the water. Someone's been waiting for you longer than most.

### EXT. THE MERE: The Jetty Head — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

At the head of the longest jetty a woman stands with her back to the Fool, pouring water
from one shallow bowl into another and back again, slow and even, the way a person tends
a small fire they do not want to lose. She is lit from within more than from without —
a soft, held light, like a lantern turned very low. When she turns, her face is gentle
and a little shy, and she does not seem surprised, only quietly, carefully glad, the way
someone is glad who has been afraid to be.

> Mind the third light along — the stem's gone crooked. It still lights. Most things do,
> with tending.

## QUEST: THE VIGIL — TENDING THE LIGHTS

### EXT. THE MERE: The Wish-Lights — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[Vigil task 1. The shoreline holds a row of wish-lanterns, all dark. The Warden walks
the row with her two bowls; the Fool follows and lights each lantern in turn — a single
unhurried interact per stem, no timing, no failure. As each catches, its flame doubles
on the flat water below. There is no reward counter and no completion chime; the lights
simply add up, warmer and warmer along the shore.]**

The Warden pours a little water at the foot of each lit stem as they go — not to douse
it, but to feed the reflection, so that every flame has its twin. She does it the way
some people say a name they're fond of: often, and without needing a reason.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE Random Lines** *(as the lights are tended; pick one at random)*

> There. That one's for a sailor's safe road home. He's three hundred years late and I
> keep it lit anyway. It's not my place to give up on his behalf.

> Some of these I know by heart. Some I never learned. You tend them the same either
> way — that's the trick nobody tells you about tending. The not-knowing is most of it.

> Slower than that. There's no last light to hurry toward. It's all just this one, and
> then the next one, and then the next.

> [If CONFESSED: Funny — you tend a light so gently, for someone who ends things. Or not
> funny. Maybe that's exactly who ought to.]

**[When the last lantern is lit, no fanfare. The whole shoreline is doubled in flame,
above and below, and the one great star sits over all of it. A long beat is held before
control returns.]**

### EXT. THE MERE: The Jetty — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[Vigil task 2. Over the course of the night, three pilgrims come down to the water,
one at a time, each unnamed. Each is a short vignette with a Fool choice set. They may

**THE FIRST PILGRIM — a small girl, out too late** *(the funny one)*

A girl of about seven comes down the shingle in a nightgown and one boot, having
plainly slipped out from somewhere, and marches up to the Warden with the enormous
seriousness only a small child brings to a genuine emergency.

**THE FIRST PILGRIM**
> I need to ask something and my mum says it's rude but I have to. Does a wish go *off*?
> Like milk? Because I made mine ages and ages ago and I can't even remember it properly
> anymore and what if it's gone bad and comes true all *wrong*.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE**
> Wishes don't go off, poppet. They only go patient. Yours is down there this minute,
> as fresh as the day you made it, waiting to be as good as you meant it.

**THE FIRST PILGRIM** *(hugely relieved, then, smaller)*
> Good. Because I can't remember making it. I only remember that I'm still waiting. That
> bit I never forget.

**THE SECOND PILGRIM — an old man with a single lantern** *(the heavy one)*

An old man comes to the third light along — the crooked one — and does not ask the
Warden anything. He tends it himself, carefully, the way a man handles the one thing he
has left. When he speaks it is half to the water and half to no one.

**THE SECOND PILGRIM**
> Made my wish the night before it all stopped. For my wife. She was ill — very ill —
> and I stood right here and wished her well with everything I had in me.

A pause. The lantern's flame doubles on the black water.

**THE SECOND PILGRIM**
> She died in my arms before the sun came up. One breath before the world forgot how to
> turn. So my wish is down there still, waiting to make a well woman of someone who's
> been three hundred years gone. It can't come true. There's no her left for it to come
> true *for*. I light the lamp anyway.

| The Fool | The old man's response |
|---|---|
| Why keep it lit, then? | Because tending it is the last of loving her I've got. The wish can't be answered. The tending still can. That's not nothing. Some nights it's very nearly enough. |

| I'm sorry. There's no fixing this. *(earnest)* | No. There isn't, and thank you for not trying. Everyone tries. You're the first to just stand here and let it be sad with me. That's the kindest anyone's been in a lifetime. |

**THE THIRD PILGRIM — a grey old soldier, out of habit** *(the plain one)*

The last to come is an old soldier, spare and grey, an ancient campaign coat buttoned
against a cold that isn't there. He does not light anything. He stands at the water with
his hands behind his back and looks at it the way a man looks at a field he once fought
over, long after anyone remembers what for.

**THE THIRD PILGRIM**
> Don't mind me. I don't wish. Gave that up before your grandmother's grandmother, and
> I've not missed it. Wishing's for people who think the world's listening.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE**
> And yet here you are. Every night I've kept count of, here you are.

**THE THIRD PILGRIM**
> Habit. Nothing more to it. Old man, old feet, old road down to the water. Means
> nothing.

He does not sound entirely as though he believes it. Neither does the water, which holds
his reflection as patiently as anyone's.

### CUT SCENE — the vigil's end

There is no dawn. The Mere has never had one and does not produce one now. But the vigil
ends all the same — every light along the shore doubled in the black water, three
pilgrims come and gone, the long still hour on the jetty sat all the way through. The
Warden of the Mere comes to where the Fool sits with Pip, and she is crying a little,
without any grief in it, the way a person cries who has held something too long and is
about to be allowed to set it down.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE**
> You stayed. Do you know — I half thought you wouldn't. They're all so used to earning
> a thing by beating it. And you just... stayed. Sat with a dog by some water and let a
> few sad people be sad. That's the whole vigil. That's all it ever was.

She kneels at the water's edge and draws from it — not a fight, no flourish, no crack of
force — a single card, wet and shining, that she holds a moment as if to be sure of it.

As she offers it, the office she has worn for three hundred years lets go. Not a break —
a settling, the way ice on a pond eases rather than shatters when the thaw comes at last.
The held light in her goes warm and ordinary and human. And a name surfaces in her, slow
and disbelieving, a wish of her own answered at the very end of a very long wait.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE**
> ...*Esther.*

She says it the way the girl couldn't remember hers and the old man couldn't say his —
tasting it, hardly daring.

**ESTHER**
> Esther. That was — yes. That's my name. I'd forgotten I was ever allowed one. Kept so
> much hope for so many people, I quite forgot to keep any for me.

**[If CONFESSED, add:]**

**ESTHER**
> And you the ending, all this while — sitting so patiently at my water. I wasn't afraid
> of you after all. You were only ever my wish, come walking down the road. The one I
> never let myself make.

She presses the card into the Fool's hands, and it is the gentlest handover in the whole
journey — no debt paid, no boss felled, a person simply giving a gift to a friend who
stayed.

**ESTHER**
> Trump the Seventeenth. Wish, it's called, and it's a true one — a light to find what
> you're looking for, a little fortune always coming back to you, and one last blossom
> on the Rose when you've nothing left. Everything I know how to give. Take it, and go on
> being the kind of Fool who stays.

END CUT SCENE

prompt, not a forced tutorial — card 17 is deep enough that the Present slot is long

### CUT SCENE — the raised staff

The Fool raises the Bindle. The Warden of the Mere sees it, and does not step back, and
does not raise the bowls, and does not so much as change her face. She only lowers her
eyes to the water, the way a person does who has decided not to watch a thing they can't
prevent.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE**
> ...Ah. All right. All right. I won't stop you. I don't know how, and I wouldn't if I
> did. The card's yours either way — it always was. I only hoped you'd let me give it
> the other way.

**[No combat encounter begins. There is no health bar, no Fortune, no music sting. The
strike lands on someone who will not defend herself, and the office lets go regardless —
because unbinding is unbinding, however it comes.]**

The light in her does not settle warm. It settles *low*. The name surfaces anyway,
because a name always does — but she says it to the water, not to the Fool, and quietly,
as if it were something she'd rather not have overheard.

**WARDEN OF THE MERE**
> ...Esther.

**ESTHER**
> Esther. There. Now I have it, and no one to say it to.

She sets the card down on the wet boards between them rather than into the Fool's hands,
and rises, and turns her low light out toward the middle of the lake. One wish-light on
the shore goes dark, all on its own, and does not come back. The water holds one fewer
star. She does not accuse the Fool of anything. She does not say another word.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. THE MERE: The Woken Water — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[If the vigil was kept:]**

Along the inlets, the dry stone wish-wells stir — a low ring of light around each rim as
the paused wishes in them wake and begin, at last, to be answerable. The one great star
over the lake does not go out, but the sky *around* it, for the first time in living
memory, begins to fill.

**[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND — night already exists across the Spread:]** The new stars come in
everywhere at once, sky-wide, over every region that has a night to show them. The whole
Spread is suddenly, quietly roofed in stars that were never there before.

`[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND: an ambient bark carries in from the shingle, per the Sun-then-Star
motif — ]`

> When the sun finally set, the stars were already waiting. Like they knew.

**[If NOT WS_SUN_UNBOUND — the Spread is still sunless and locked at its various frozen
hours:]** The Mere's own eternal night fills with stars immediately — it never needed a
day to have a night. Elsewhere, the change is set but held: the other regions have no
night yet to show it in. The stars are *there*, in the world-state, waiting on the Sun
the way the wishes waited on the Star. When `WS_SUN_UNBOUND` fires later, they arrive
all at once, already in place.

`[If NOT WS_SUN_UNBOUND: the Sun-not-yet motif is available to Noonlands barks after the
fact — ]`

> First true night we ever had, and not one star in it. Got them later, mind.

Esther stands at the jetty head and watches her lake begin to answer people again, and
looks, for the first time, like someone who has set a very heavy thing down and is
surprised to find her arms still work.

**[If the vigil was attacked:]**

The wish-wells wake — the flag fires the same, the wishes stir the same, the sky fills
the same, and the Sun/Star order variants above apply identically; the world does not
withhold its hope on account of how the Star was freed. But the Mere itself is lessened.
One wish-light on the shore stays dark, permanently. The jetty, from this night on, is
tended by no one; where three pilgrims came and were heard, there is now a single thin
bark pool and a great deal of quiet. Esther is not here. No line marks any of it. The
region simply keeps the memory in how empty it is.

### EXT. THE MERE: The Shingle — NIGHT

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The old soldier is buttoning his campaign coat to go, the new stars coming in over the
water behind him. He stops beside the Fool on his way up the shingle.

**THE THIRD PILGRIM**
> So that's that, then. The wishes are awake. Everyone down here's over the moon about
> it — you'd think the war was won.

A pause. He looks at the woken wells without much love.

**THE THIRD PILGRIM**
> I'll tell you what you've done, since no one else will say it plain. You've made it so
> a wish can come true. Which means, from tonight, a wish can also *not* come true. All
> those years the lake couldn't answer anybody — nobody down here ever got refused.
> Safest hope in the world, a hope that only waits. You went and made it dangerous
> again.

**THE FOOL**
> Wasn't that better than never being answered?

**THE THIRD PILGRIM**
> [If NOT CONFESSED: For most of them. Not for all of them. There's some of us liked the
> waiting because the waiting couldn't break your heart. You've handed a lot of people
> back the chance to be disappointed. That's a real thing you did. Kind, mostly. Not
> kind to everyone.]
>
> [If CONFESSED: For most of them — and it won't last, will it. I know what your cards
> add up to. You'll wake every wish on this water and then you'll end the world they'd
> come true in. Paused hope was kinder. It cost nobody anything. But you're not in the
> business of kinder. You're in the business of *finished*. I've been a soldier. I know
> the difference, and I know which one takes more nerve. Go on.]

He touches his cap, not unkindly, and walks up the road, out of habit, back the way he
always comes and always goes. He does not look at the new stars once.

## Open questions

- **Vigil pacing (carried from outline).** "One night" still needs a concrete pacing
  target at implementation so the vigil reads as restful, not padded. The script fixes
  the *shape* (three tasks, any order, an explicitly un-timed-out sit-with-Pip beat that
  the player leaves at will), but the real-time budget for the sit beat and the spacing
  of the three pilgrim arrivals is a tuning call. Recommend the sit beat have no upper
  bound and no nudge whatsoever, and the pilgrims be spaced by player movement along the
  shore rather than a clock.
- **Attack-path late callback (carried from outline).** Whether the permanently dimmer
  Mere ever earns an *optional* far-later NPC mention, or whether silence serves "the
  game just remembers" better. This script commits to silence — the only acknowledgment
  is the region's own emptiness and a single flat bark pool. Recommend keeping it that
  way; flagging in case a later region wants to reference a Fool who struck the Star.
- **Pilgrim distinctness vs. the side quests (tension flagged, resolved in-script).**
  SQ-MERE-02 flagged that its named pilgrim Thomlin Reeve (waiting on a wish "made the
  night before the Stall," for *forgiveness*) overlapped with the outline's unnamed
  "widower." Resolved here by giving the vigil's second pilgrim a *distinct* wish — for
  his late wife's recovery, made the night before the Stall, which *cannot* be answered
  because she died that same night — leaving Thomlin's answerable forgiveness-wish, and
  his reveal, wholly intact. Both are unnamed-vs-named and thematically echoing but not
  interchangeable. Confirm the echo reads as intentional, not as a duplicated NPC.
- **Proposed NPCs no longer need promotion.** The outline proposed promoting the three
  pilgrims (old soldier, widower, child) to `characters.md` before script status. The
  decision to keep all three *unnamed and single-night* (per the mandate to protect the
  side quests' named pilgrims) removes that requirement — as unnamed ambient roles they
  introduce no new canon. If any is ever named or made recurring, it would then need a
  `characters.md` entry in the same change.

# MQ16 — Already Falling

## Introduction

The player comes to the Spire — the broken tooth the Fool first saw from the Cliff, a
tower struck by lightning three centuries ago and frozen mid-collapse, rubble hanging in
the air, staircases ending in open sky. It is a single vertical setpiece with no branch:
the ascent through the frozen catastrophe *is* the fight. At the base sits the undertown,
a ring of houses built in permanent flinch-distance, and its bell-keeper, Sorrel Vance,
who will beg the player to reconsider — not for the Fool's sake, but for a skyline she and
everyone she knows navigates by. The climb teaches a vertical traversal grammar on the
stable lower tiers, then turns lethal as the Warden's lightning re-awakens gravity in
patches, dropping whole staircases the moment before they're used; the strikes read as a
learnable rota, not chaos. At the summit waits the Warden, a crowned figure fighting
bare-handed for a ruin, in a duel that is short and desperate on purpose. Unbinding does
not end with an "after" up here — the Tower finally falls *with the Fool aboard*, a
scripted ride to the ground where players holding Overturn (feather-fall) or Triumph (the
Chariot mount) get style options and everyone else lands safe by other means. The Spire
requires no prior unbinding, so every scene is written to be reached in any order, and —
because MQ16 sits deep in Act III for most players — every key beat carries an
`[If CONFESSED]` variant: by the time most Fools arrive, the undertown already suspects
what an unbinding costs. When it's done, the skyline changes for every region in the game,
debris opens new cave routes, and thunderstorms join the weather — and the fall lands
real, non-metaphorical masonry on a town that only ever half-believed it could.

## QUEST: ALREADY FALLING

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Undertown — OVERCAST, STORM-THREAT (state-dependent)

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The Fool comes up a switchback road into the undertown — a ring of narrow houses all
built with the same architectural flinch, roofs pitched away from the tower, doors and
windows facing anywhere but up. Overhead, filling half the sky, hangs the Spire: a tower
snapped through the middle three hundred years ago and frozen in the act of coming down,
its upper storeys leaning out over the town, whole staircases ending in air, chunks of
masonry hung motionless where they fell. A cornice the size of a wagon floats above the
market square, and nobody in the market square looks at it.

[If WS_SUN_UNBOUND: night has been let back into the world, and the frozen tower is a
black silhouette against real stars — the townsfolk navigate by its notch on the horizon
the way sailors read a coastline.]

**[Player regains control. A soft highlight marks the town well and, beyond it, a squat
belfry at the town's heart.]**

Pip trots ahead, stops, and sits — head tilted straight up at the hanging cornice with
the frank, unbothered interest of a dog who can plainly see the thing the people around
him have agreed not to.

**THE QUERENT**
> There it is up close, then — the fall that never finished. Three hundred years it's
> been leaning over these good people's breakfast, and three hundred years they've
> managed not to mention it. Remarkable, what a person can learn to live directly
> beneath.

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Market Square — OVERCAST

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The square hums with the small business of a town that has decided, collectively and
permanently, that everything is fine. A rope-and-pulley contraption stands bolted to the
belfry: the **lightning bell**, a warning system rigged generations back for the strike
everyone's grandparents expected any minute. Its rope is frayed grey with age. Its
striking-hammer is cocked and waiting. Nobody has touched it in living memory, because the
strike it warns of has been in the middle of happening since before anyone alive was born.

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Lower Tiers — OVERCAST

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

A ramp of fallen roof-slate leads up into the tower's stable base — the first storeys,
which lean but do not float, solid enough to teach a body how to climb the rest. The stone
here is warm to the touch and faintly humming, as if the whole ruin were a struck bell
still deciding whether to finish its note.

**[Tutorial prompt: mantle a handhold — grip-marked ledges the eye can read.]**

**[Tutorial prompt: timed rubble shift — a hanging slab drifts a hand's width on a slow,
readable beat; cross when it's settled, not when it's moving. This is the grammar the
ascent will demand at speed later.]**

The Fool works up three tiers of leaning masonry, learning the tower's two verbs: hold on,
and read the beat. No lightning here yet. The catastrophe is only ever a floor above.

**THE QUERENT**
> Feel that hum? That's three hundred years of *nearly*. The whole tower is one long held
> breath — and up top there's a man whose entire job is not letting it out. You're going
> to go and change his mind. Or he yours. One of the two, and only one way to find out.

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Frozen Ascent — OVERCAST, LIGHTNING (state-dependent)

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Past the broken landing the tower comes apart around the Fool: staircases hang in
mid-topple, whole rooms float unmoored, a chandelier the size of a cottage drifts sideways
through open air. And now the lightning wakes — not from the sky, but from the Warden
far above, forked light crawling *down* the ruin in patches, and wherever it touches,
gravity remembers itself. A stair the Fool was a stride from using goes white, then
falls, three centuries of stalled collapse finishing in half a second and gone into the
haze below.

**[Traversal-combat: the ascent proper. Suspended rubble is the level geometry; the
Warden's lightning re-awakens gravity in marked patches, dropping the platform an instant
after it's lit. The climber's rope, the mantles, and the read-the-beat timing from the
lower tiers are now demanded together and at speed.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> He can feel you climbing. That's him doing that — waking the fall in bits, dropping the
> floor out from under you sooner than let one honest inch of this tower reach the ground.
> Three hundred years of practice at exactly one thing: *not yet.*

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Storm-Tier — LIGHTNING

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Higher, the strikes stop being scattered and start being a *pattern*. The lightning crawls
the ruin on a rota — a sweep of forked light left to right, a beat, a sweep back — slow
enough to learn, punishing only to the impatient. The tower is a metronome of near-misses,
and the Fool climbs on its offbeats.

**[Traversal-combat: the lightning telegraphs on a fixed, learnable cadence (the
card-first rule — sudden upheaval, mastered). Platforms drop on the sweep; the safe route
is always the beat between. Overturn's feather-fall Past-slot, if held, turns a mistimed
drop from lethal into survivable — an optional mercy, not a requirement.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> There — you've got it. It was never random, the sky's temper. Nothing's random up here.
> A strike is only an ending that lost its nerve and struck early. Learn its rhythm and
> you can climb straight up the middle of a catastrophe. Which is, roughly, the entire job
> description.

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Summit — LIGHTNING

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The last drift of rubble sets the Fool onto the summit: a shattered crown of a room, open
to the sky on every side, its floor a slab of throne-hall hanging over a very long way
down. At its centre stands the WARDEN — a tall, gaunt figure in the rotted finery of a
king, a real crown gone green with age still on his brow, hands empty and raised like a
man holding up a ceiling only he can see. He was king of this tower the night the
lightning struck. He has not let go of it since.

He turns, and looks at the Fool, and something in the ruined face is almost — glad.

CUT SCENE

**THE WARDEN**
> Up. All the way up. Do you know how long it has been since anyone came *up?* They flee a
> falling thing. Every soul I ever knew fled it. And here is one who climbed the fall to
> reach me.

**THE FOOL**
> I came for the fall.

**THE WARDEN**
> *For* it. [A bark of something that isn't quite a laugh.] Then you're the first honest
> visitor this height has had. Everyone else came to plead. To prop. To tell me the tower
> could hold if I only held it harder. Nobody ever came to help it *land.*

He lowers his hands a fraction, and the whole summit groans, and he snaps them up again,
and it stills. Three hundred years of holding a ceiling by main will.

**THE WARDEN**
> [If CONFESSED: You've been to the others, haven't you. Turning them loose. I know the
> look. Good. I have been the last thing in this world refusing to finish, and I am so
> tired of being right.]
>
> No throne here. No court. No stone that hasn't already broken. Only the will not to
> let it end — and you, come to break *that* too. Well. Try. Please. It has been so long
> since anyone made me let go.

END CUT SCENE

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Summit Duel — LIGHTNING

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[Boss duel — short and desperate on purpose. The Warden fights bare-handed, a crowned
man swinging for a ruin, drawing the tower's lightning through his own body to strike and
to wake the floor out from under the Fool in turn. No weapon, no loot, no phases of
attrition — a brief, furious, human fight. He is not defending a throne; he is holding up
a fall, and each blow he takes is one more grip he can't keep. The tower loses a storey to
the haze with every exchange.]**

**THE WARDEN** *(mid-duel, over the collapsing floor)*
> Feel that? That's a hundred years going. And that — two hundred. You're not beating me,
> Fool. You're *helping* me drop it. I can't tell anymore which of us wants this more.

The Fool lands the last blow. The Warden staggers, catches himself on nothing, and the
crown slips down over one eye — and for the first time in three centuries he does not push
the tower back up. He lets his hands fall. Everything the hands were holding begins,
finally, to move.

### CUT SCENE

The summit tilts. Not a lurch — a slow, enormous *leaning*, the whole Spire beginning the
last of a fall it started before anyone alive was born. And in the Warden's face the
office cracks — the king, the crown, the three hundred years of refusal splitting like
old plaster, and underneath it a plain, exhausted man surfacing into his own name.

**THE WARDEN**
> Oh. *Oh.* It's — it's letting go. After all this. It was always going to feel like—

He stops. Something rises under the crack, the way a held breath finally lets go.

**THE WARDEN**
> ...*Balen.*

He says it into the wind of the falling tower, tasting it.

**BALEN**
> Balen. That was — that *is* me. Not the Warden. Balen. I had a name before I had a
> ceiling to hold, and I have spent every year of the hold forgetting it.

The floor drops another degree. There is no ground to reach any more, no room to stand and
finish a conversation. Balen understands this before the Fool does, and reaches out — no
throne to leave from, no ceremony to be had at the end of the world's longest fall — and
presses a card into the Fool's hand as the summit comes apart between them.

**BALEN**
> [If CONFESSED: You know what your climbing costs. You've known for regions. Then you
> know I don't say this lightly: thank you. I could not have set it down alone. I had
> forgotten there was a down.]
>
> Trump the Sixteenth. Ruin, they'll name it, if it's named at all. Take it — quickly,
> there's no *after* up here, we say our goodbyes on the way down or not at all. You came
> looking for the fall. Ride it with me.

### EXT. THE SPIRE: The Undertown, After — CLEARING, FIRST REAL WEATHER

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The Fool lands — hard, safe, breathless — in the settling dust of a tower that has, at
long last, finished falling. Where the Spire stood is a low, smoking cairn of its own
rubble and, above it, for the first time in three hundred years, *ordinary sky* — grey,
moving, weather in it. The broken tooth is gone from the horizon. It is gone from every
horizon; the change reaches as far as anyone can see, and further.

Balen sits in the rubble he spent three centuries holding up, alive, ordinary, breathing
the plain air of a man who has finally set something down. He does not vanish. He does not
crumble. He survives the fall he at last allowed — a tired man in a ruined coat, no crown,
no office, blinking at a sky he forgot could move.

**BALEN**
> It moves. The sky *moves.* I'd forgotten it did that. [He watches a cloud cross for a
> long moment.] I don't know what a man does, after he's finished being a fall. I expect
> I'll find out slowly. That's rather the luxury of it. Slowly.

**[The fall's real cost is visible at the near edge of the undertown: flinch-distance held
for most, but not all — masonry has come down across the town's uphill houses, and figures
are already digging in the dust. This is staged but NOT resolved here; SQ-SPIRE-02 (active
on WS_TOWER_UNBOUND) owns the family who lost their home and Harrow Brock the bell-watch
veteran. MQ16 only establishes that the fall landed real weight on real roofs.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> Not clean, was it. It rarely is, a thing this size coming down. Look there — the fall
> didn't read the property lines. Somebody down the hill's lost a roof to the very stone
> they spent their lives insisting couldn't reach them. That's the bill for an ending. It
> always comes, and it never comes to the person who earned it.

### INT./EXT. THE SPIRE: The Belfry, After — CLEARING

CUT SCENE

Sorrel Vance stands in her swept belfry before the lightning bell — the warning system for
a strike that will now, forever, never come, because the thing it feared has happened and
is over. The tower it faced is a shape on the ground. Her hand rests on the frayed grey
rope.

She rings it. Once. For a tower that isn't there to hear it.

**SORREL VANCE**
> There. Rung at last. For the strike, or the fall, or the notch that isn't on the
> horizon any more — I couldn't tell you which. A bell doesn't know the difference. It
> just says: *something happened here.* Somebody ought to say that. Somebody ought to.
>
> [If CONFESSED: I rang it early, you know. Before you'd even reached the top. Just to
> have said something first. To have got one word in before the world changed its shape
> on me without asking. Silly. But it's mine, that ringing. The world can have the rest.]

She lets the rope go. The single note carries out over a town relearning its own skyline,
and she does not cry, quite, and she does not smile, quite, and she stands with her hand
where the rope was, mourning a silhouette and not a monster.

END CUT SCENE

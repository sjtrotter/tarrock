# MQ07 — The Triumph

## Introduction

The player reaches the Longroad, the great ring-causeway circling the Axis, and finds a
war-train still marching a triumph three centuries after its war ended — banners bleached
white, trumpets dented past tune, momentum long since curdled past the point of meaning
anything. Nothing on this road stops, which is the whole problem: the procession never
arrives, and no one marching it remembers what it won. To unbind the Chariot the Fool
must catch the galloping column on foot (no mounts exist yet — the Chariot itself is the
game's first mount), leap aboard from a toll-fort's gate-arm, and fight the length of it
along carriage roofs and banner rigging to the Charioteer at the head. He cannot be beaten
with the Bindle: he fights one-handed because the reins are in the other, and the only way
through is to take those reins and *pull*. The halt is the unbinding. Completing MQ07 lights
the Waystation network for fast travel across the whole Spread and grants Trump VII, which
answers as a summonable mount — so this quest is also the moment the map opens up. Two
locals frame the change: Corporal Pike, a marching soldier resting at a toll-fort, and Widow
Tallow, whose roadside inn was built entirely on feeding a parade that will never pass her
door again. If the player reaches the Longroad after MQ13 (Death, "An Ending"), the key
scenes below carry their `[If CONFESSED: …]` variants.

## QUEST: THE TRIUMPH

### EXT. THE LONGROAD: The Ring-Causeway — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The road the Fool steps onto is wider than any road has a right to be — a raised stone
causeway, four wagons abreast, curving away in both directions until the curve becomes a
ring and the ring becomes a rumour of distance. To the inward side, far off and centred on
everything, a single silver point turns, turns, turns: the Axis, which the whole causeway
exists to circle and never reach.

And on the road, always on the road, there is dust. A long banner of it, kicked up by
something moving fast and moving away, circling the Axis exactly as it has for three hundred
years. The player cannot see the front of the procession or the back of it. Only the dust,
and under the dust a sound felt in the stone underfoot before it is heard — the tramp of
feet that have never once broken step, and a drum keeping a cadence with no verse left in
it.

**[Tutorial prompt: the causeway curves both ways; a soft highlight marks the nearer
toll-fort, upstream of the dust.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> The Longroad, little Excuse. Every road you've walked so far feeds into this one
> eventually — it's the world's spine, and it has been marching in a circle since before
> anyone thought to ask it where it was going. Mind the dust. There's a whole army inside
> it, and it is very much not stopping to say hello.

### EXT. THE LONGROAD: Pike's Toll-Fort — DAY

raised over the causeway and a brazier gone cold beside it. It is built to make travellers

step with his boots off and his helmet upturned in his lap like a bowl.

### EXT. THE LONGROAD: The Chase — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The dust arrives ahead of the column and then the column arrives inside the dust: rank on
rank of marchers, a river of parade steel pouring past the fort at a pace no walking has any
business keeping. The tail of it — the last wagons, the stragglers, the baggage-carts —
sweeps by, already leaving.

### EXT. THE LONGROAD: The Gate-Arm Leap — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The near fort's gate-arm hangs out over the road at the perfect wrong height — a
counterweighted beam, raised, level, close enough to the passing carriage roofs to be an
invitation and a threat in one. Below it the column thunders past, roofs and rigging and
running-boards flickering by in the gaps.

**[Tutorial prompt: climb the gate-arm and time a leap onto a passing carriage roof. The
timing window is generous but unforgiving of the wrong roof — a low baggage-cart, not the
high banner-wagons.]**

The first leap comes up short. The Fool jumps for a carriage that is already gone, hits the
running-board of the next with both hands, and is dragged three yards along the causeway
stone by their own grip before losing it — tumbling clear, rolling, unhurt, watching the
column pull away again with the specific fury of a near miss.

**[Tutorial prompt: recover, climb the *next* fort's gate-arm, and leap again. This time a
banner-wagon's rigging line hangs low and trailing — catch the line, not the roof.]**

The second leap the hand finds rope. A trailing rigging line, frayed and three centuries
weathered, cracks taut under the Fool's weight — and holds. Pip lands neat as a cat on the
roof above, already aboard, already looking back as if to ask what took so long.

The Fool is on the war-train. The world of solid, stopped ground drops behind, and
everything now is moving, and will not stop moving until somebody makes it.

### EXT. THE WAR-TRAIN: The Rear Carriages — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

Up close, the triumph is a corpse that forgot to fall down. The banners overhead — great
regimental colours, once every shade a proud army owns — have bleached to a uniform bone
white, the devices on them worn to ghosts of themselves. The gilt on the carriages has gone
to green. A trumpeter stands frozen mid-fanfare on a passing roof, the bell of his
instrument dented so deep it could never sound again, and his cheeks puffed for a note three
hundred years unblown.

And the marchers hold the roofs. Parade-armoured Blanks — human-shaped, blank oval faces,
tabards bearing the marching column's higher ranks in colours long since faded to grey —
patrol the carriage-tops in perfect drill, wheeling and presenting arms to an inspecting
officer who will never come, on a march with nowhere on earth left to go.

**[Player regains control on the moving roofs. The whole fight takes place on a train at
full gallop: roofs, rigging, running-boards, all sliding underfoot.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> There's your welcome party. Same Blanks you know, dressed for the occasion — parade
> armour, higher cards on the tabards, no more mouths than any of the rest. Fell one and
> its card blows straight off the back of the train, gone into the dust. Nothing ends out
> here, remember. It only relocates.

The first Blanks turn from their phantom inspection and level parade-halberds at the Fool.
They come on in step, of course. They have never done anything else.

**THE QUERENT** *(mid-fight, easy)*
> Forward. Always forward — it's the only direction this whole army understands, so borrow
> it. Don't try to hold ground; there isn't any. There's just the next roof, and the next,
> and the man at the front who hasn't turned round in three hundred years.

The banner rigging, climbed, gives a moment above the whole procession: the column stretches
away ahead, carriage after carriage after carriage, and at the very head of it, small with
distance and haloed in his own dust, a single figure stands at the reins and does not look
back.

### EXT. THE WAR-TRAIN: The Charioteer's Box — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

The head of the train is a high open chariot-platform, more throne than driver's box, and on
it stands THE CHARIOTEER: tall in tarnished ceremonial armour, a laurel gone black on his
brow, one gauntleted fist wrapped white-knuckled in a great fistful of reins that run
forward into the dust to a team the Fool cannot see. His other hand holds a cavalry sabre,
loose and expert and entirely unbothered.

He hears the Fool come up the last carriage. He does not turn his head — the reins do not
permit it — but he speaks, forward, into the wind, in a voice built for carrying down a mile
of marching men.

CUT SCENE

**THE CHARIOTEER**
> A runner. Good. About time the column got fresh legs — the last recruit's been marching
> so long he's forgotten his own name, and so, if we're honest, has the man in front of
> him.

*(The sabre comes up, still without a backward glance.)*

> You want a place in this triumph? Earn it. Come forward. Everyone in this column came
> forward once. It's the coming to a halt that none of us ever managed.

END CUT SCENE

GAMEPLAY: The Charioteer fights one-handed — the sabre only, quick and disciplined and
punishing, the rein-fist never once loosening. The fight is as much closing distance against
a man who is always half a carriage ahead as it is landing blows: he gives ground up the
platform, along the traces, never toward the Fool, always deeper into the procession's own
momentum, and every clean sabre parry that ought to have been a killing riposte instead
simply keeps the reins where they are.

**THE CHARIOTEER** *(mid-fight)*
> Better. Much better. But you're fighting the wrong war, runner — you keep striking for a
> finish, and there is no finish. There's only the road, and the road goes round.

The Fool lands the blow that should end it — a full committed strike, clean through the
Charioteer's guard — and it changes nothing. The man rocks, recovers, and drives forward
another stride, sabre back up, entirely undefeated, because a sabre was never the thing
holding this together.

**[Beat: the realization. The rein-fist glows to the Fool's eye — the White Rose stirs
toward it — the one thing on the whole train that isn't moving relative to itself. The
Bindle will not win this. The reins will.]**

**THE QUERENT**
> You can't cut him loose, little Excuse. Nobody's holding *him* — he's holding the road,
> and he'll hold it till his arm comes off before he lets it stop on a sword-stroke.
> There's only one way this parade ever halts, and it's the same way it started. Somebody's
> hand on the reins. Take them. *Take them.*

### EXT. THE WAR-TRAIN: The Reins — DAY

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

**[Setpiece: close the last of the distance and seize the reins directly from the
Charioteer's free hand. This becomes a sustained grab-and-pull — the Fool's grip closing
over his around the leather, and then a long, escalating haul against three hundred years of
gathered momentum. The train fights the pull. The road fights the pull. The Charioteer,
appalled, at first fights the pull too — and then, fractionally, stops. See Open questions
re: the input rig for this beat.]**

The reins come alive in the Fool's hands the instant they close over the Charioteer's — a
living, dragging weight, the pull of an entire army's forward want, three centuries deep and
still accelerating. For a moment the Fool is simply lifted onto their toes by it, hauled
along, losing.

Then Pip sets his paws and adds his own small furious weight to the line, and the Fool digs
in, and the Charioteer — for the first time in three hundred years — feels the reins go
*backward* in his hand.

**THE CHARIOTEER** *(no longer forward — turning, at last, to look at who's stopping him)*
> No. No — not yet — just a little *further*, we're nearly — we have always been nearly
> there —

**[Escalating pull. The Fool wins it by inches, and only barely.]**

TRANSITION TO CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE — the halt

The reins come back.

And three hundred years of momentum begin, at last, to die.

It does not stop all at once. It cannot — there is too much of it, too long a run of it, and
the halt goes down the length of the train like a held breath finally let out: the lead
chariot's wheels lock and *skid*, screaming a long bright line down the causeway stone, and
behind it carriage after carriage shudders and slews and grinds, the whole column
telescoping down into a slow, enormous, unstoppable stop.

The dust that has hidden this procession for three centuries comes forward, overtakes it,
and hangs — and then begins, softly, to *settle*. The banners overhead, deprived all at once
of the wind that kept them flying, fold. One after another down the mile of the train they
lose their shape and come down, bone-white, over the carriages, like sheets drawn up over
furniture in a house being closed for the last time.

The drum stops. The cadence that was under everything, that Corporal Pike could feel in his
own feet from a fort away, stops — and the silence it leaves is the loudest thing on the
Longroad in three hundred years.

The train, at the very end, gives one last long groaning skid — and is still.

END CUT SCENE

### CUT SCENE — the unbinding

The Charioteer stands in the settling dust with the reins slack in his hands and the Fool's
hands still over them, and he is looking at the halted train the way a man looks at
something he was certain could not exist.

The office cracks first. The black laurel, the tarnished ceremonial armour, the whole hard
carriage of *the General* — it comes apart at the seams the way the halt came down the train,
front to back, and underneath it is only a soldier, an old one, appallingly tired.

**THE CHARIOTEER**
> It stopped. By the Deal — it *stopped*. Three hundred years I held it forward because a
> commander does not halt a triumph, a commander does not — and it turns out all it ever
> wanted was for someone to pull the other way.

Something surfaces then, the way a name surfaces — not injury, not defeat, just the man
under the office coming back up for air. His grip loosens on the reins. His eyes clear.

**THE CHARIOTEER**
> ...*Cassian.*

He says it the way a man says a word in a language he was born to and hasn't heard spoken
aloud in centuries.

**CASSIAN**
> Cassian. That's — yes. That was the name before the rank ate it. Before I was *the
> General*, before I was *the Charioteer*, before I was three miles of dust going nowhere.
> Cassian. Gods, the *sound* of it.

*(He looks down the length of the halted train — the folded banners, the stilled marchers,
the drummer's raised sticks come to rest — and the horror of it and the relief of it arrive
in him at the same instant.)*

**CASSIAN**
> [If CONFESSED: I know what you are, runner. Word reaches even a column that never stops —
> you're the one soldier still capable of *halting*, in the small sense and the very large
> one. You're going to bring the whole world to the stop I just felt. ...I find I can't be
> sorry. Not with this quiet in my hands. Someone ought to have pulled the other way on all
> of it, long ago.]
>
> Do you understand what you've done? You've let it *arrive*. Three centuries I marched a
> victory that had had its destination cut out of it — momentum with nowhere to be, which is
> the cruellest thing you can do to a man who only ever knew how to advance. And you simply —
> stopped. Turned round. Looked at me. I'd forgotten a person could.

He unwraps the reins from his hand — the leather has left three hundred years of itself
pressed into his palm — and where they were is a card, warm, held out to the Fool with the
reins still warm in both their hands.

**CASSIAN**
> Trump VII. Triumph, if anyone writes it down. Take it — and take this with it: it will
> answer when you call, and carry you anywhere you please, at any speed you like. But it
> only ever learned one hard lesson, same as me. *Stopping is the difficult part.* Mind
> that, and you'll not end up a General.

The causeway is a different world stopped than it ever was moving. Where the dust used to

### EXT. THE LONGROAD: Widow Tallow's Inn — DUSK

THIRD PERSON GAMEPLAY

A little way round the ring stands a roadside inn — long, low, and built wrong, the player
slowly realises: built to serve a road going *past*, not a road going anywhere. Its whole
front is a serving-counter open to the causeway, a hundred tin cups on hooks, a great cold
range built to feed a marching band every single evening at the same hour on the same lap,
forever.

WIDOW TALLOW stands behind the counter with a ladle in her hand and nothing in the pot,
watching the mile of stopped train the way you watch weather that has just done something it
has never done before.

**WIDOW TALLOW Random Lines** *(repeatable; pick one at random)*

> Every evening for three hundred years the band came past at the same bar of the same
> tune, and I had a hundred cups poured and waiting. They never stopped to drink them. But
> they came *past*. You could set your grief by it.

> There'll be caravans, they tell me. New trade, new faces, coin that doesn't march in a
> circle. And I'm glad of it, I am. It's only — it isn't *them*. It'll never again be them.

> [If CONFESSED: I know what you're doing, love, up and down the Spread. I know where this
> road of yours ends, and whose door it stops at last. I'll not curse you for it. Someone
> pours the last cup for everyone eventually. Turns out it's you.]

### CUT SCENE

**WIDOW TALLOW**

# SQ-MERE-03 — The Pilgrim Who Wished Wrong

## Introduction

A light, dry, warm-hearted quest that trades on the Mere's absolute gentleness: because
nothing here can hurt anyone — no Fortune, no Blanks, no combat, and, before the Star is
unbound, no wish that can actually come true — the stakes of this quest are entirely
emotional, and entirely comic. A young pilgrim has been carrying three hundred years of
mortification over a wish she made in a moment of petty spite, and wants desperately to take
it back before it can ever land. There is no danger in helping her. There is only the small,
excellent comedy of un-wishing, and the very old, very real embarrassment of finally saying
a mean thing out loud so you can be done with it.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** Betsy Marrow (canon, `characters.md` §Regional named NPCs), young and visibly cringing, pulls the Fool aside at the jetty. She has a
   confession, and she would like to make it to a stranger with a dog rather than anyone
   who'll still be here tomorrow.
2. **The confession.** Three hundred years ago, in a fit of sisterly spite over something
   tiny, she dropped a wish into the Mere *against* her sister — small, mean, the kind of thing
   you'd never say sober. She forgave her sister within the week. The wish, however, is still
   down there, paused with everything else, and Betsy has spent three centuries mortified that
   it might one day wake up and come true on a grudge she no longer holds.
3. **The request.** She begs the Fool to help her retract it before the wells can ever stir.
   (Pre-unbinding: the wishes are safely paused, so the whole errand is harmless — which she
   half-knows and cannot make herself believe.)
4. **The un-wishing.** There is no ritual for taking a wish back, so the Fool and Betsy have to
   improvise one — fishing the right coin from among centuries of others, or composing a
   solemn counter-wish, or simply saying the mean thing aloud to the lake and then saying sorry
   to it. The comedy is in the logistics; the lake bears it all with serene indifference.
5. **The honest beat.** What Betsy is actually doing, it becomes clear, is forgiving *herself*
   — she settled things with her sister centuries ago, but never with the version of herself
   who was petty enough to wish it. The retraction is really an apology she owed no one but her
   own memory.
6. **Closing beat.** Betsy can look at the lake without flinching now, which she hasn't managed
   in three hundred years. She's still embarrassed. She's just no longer haunted.
   - `[If WS_STAR_UNBOUND: the wells have already woken and Betsy is beside herself — did it`
     `come true? It did not: the Mere's gentleness fizzled a spiteful wish to nothing long ago,`
     `and the "danger" was only ever her own conscience. The relief and the anticlimax land`
     `together, which is the joke.]`

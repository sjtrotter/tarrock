# SQ-LONGROAD-02 — The Toll-Keeper Left Behind

## Introduction

For three hundred years Grubb Farrow collected the same toll from the same procession as
it circled past his fort — a captive audience that had to stop, and had to listen, every
single lap. He filled the wait with terrible jokes and genuinely superb hand-drawn maps.
Then the Fool halted the Chariot, the procession dispersed, and the caravans and fast-
travelling strangers that now use the Longroad have no reason on earth to stop at Grubb's
gate. The player meets a man who is, very late in a very long life, learning the hardest
lesson the freed road teaches: how to be worth stopping for when nobody is obliged to.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** Post-`WS_CHARIOT_UNBOUND`, Grubb Farrow (`characters.md` §Regional named
   NPCs — promoted in the parallel slate change) still stands at his toll-fort with the
   bar up and nothing to bar. Merchant caravans roll straight past; travellers arriving
   by Waystation don't even come down his stretch of road. He greets the Fool with the
   full, rusty, three-centuries-rehearsed toll-keeper's patter — jokes first, then the
   toll — and is visibly thrown when the Fool can simply keep walking.
2. **The complication.** Grubb doesn't want charity and won't take a toll he hasn't
   earned. What he has is the maps: three hundred years of the Longroad and its spurs,
   annotated in a hand that knows every ford and every bad culvert on the ring. He's
   never once considered that a map is worth more to a person who *can* leave than to a
   parade that only ever went in circles.
3. **The turn.** The Fool can carry Grubb's maps to a passing caravan-master, or coax
   Grubb himself down to the nearest active Waystation to meet the strangers arriving
   there — either way engineering the thing he can't do for himself: a person choosing to
   stop and talk to him with no gate between them.
   - Fool (earnest): "Your maps are the road's memory." / (foolish): "Charge them for the
     jokes. They're worse."
4. **The ache.** The first traveller who stops does so because the map is genuinely
   good, not because they had to — and Grubb, who has never been chosen in his life, only
   ever *required*, doesn't know where to put his hands. The moment is small and it
   unmoors him.
5. **The laugh.** He tries a toll-keeper's joke on a listener free to walk away mid-
   punchline, panics, and finishes it anyway to an empty stretch of road; the traveller,
   three steps gone, calls the punchline back over their shoulder — got it, kept it,
   chose to.
6. **Closing beat.** Grubb repurposes the fort as a mapmaker's stall by the road, or
   hangs up the toll-bar and takes the Waystation-keeper's Calling greeting arrivals — a
   modest, unheroic act of becoming someone people stop for on purpose. His barks shift
   from rote toll-patter to a mapmaker glad of company.

# SQ-WHEELHOUSE-02 — The Croupier's Tell

## Introduction

In a city that assumes every table is rigged, one dealer has run a perfectly fair game
for three hundred years and taken quiet, enormous pride in it. "Honest" Marrow Vance will
teach the Fool the craft of straight play — the difference between a good bluff and an
honest tell — for no reward but the pleasure of a student who actually listens. This is a
teaching quest, not a fight: a gentle skill-and-character piece that pairs naturally with
the Croupier Calling and rewards the player who wants to understand the Wheelhouse rather
than merely win money off it. It plays before or after the Wheel turns, and the turning
changes what fairness even *means* to Marrow — the quest notices.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** At his table (on the line between the districts, where he insists on
   sitting), Marrow Vance (canon, `characters.md` §Regional named NPCs) clocks the Fool immediately as someone with no angle — a stranger
   with a dog, not a mark and not a cheat. He offers, unprompted, to teach. Coins-culture
   through and through: measured, transactional, and drier than the cards.
2. **The first lesson — the tell.** A small reading contest: Marrow reads the Fool while
   the Fool learns to read him. The lesson is that a *tell* is honest information the body
   gives away for free, and reading it is not cheating — cheating is manufacturing what
   the cards don't say.
3. **The second lesson — the bluff.** Marrow demonstrates his whole ethic by catching a
   genuine cheat at a neighboring table, without a scene, with one unhurried sentence. The
   cheat is spectacularly bad at it (the comedy); Marrow doesn't gloat, just resets the
   deck and moves on (the honest beat) — his pride is in the clean game, never in the
   catch.
4. **The graduation hand.** The Fool plays one straight hand with Marrow, win or lose. The
   point is that it was fair; the outcome is beside it. Marrow is satisfied either way.
5. **[If WS_FORTUNE_UNBOUND: the meaning shifts.]** With the Wheel turning and odds finally
   real, Marrow is briefly thrown — for three centuries "fair" meant *unrigged in a rigged
   place*, a lonely virtue no one could verify; now it means an even chance in a living
   game anyone can check. He re-runs the graduation hand with real stakes and real luck in
   the room, delighted to find his pride survived the change and simply grew up.
6. **Resolution.** Marrow declares the Fool a fit student and marks it the only way a Coins
   man would — a small honest wage and a nod. The Croupier Calling, if the Fool takes it
   up, gains his workplace-memory lines.

# SQ-WANDS-01 — The Trial Piece

## Introduction

Homed in the Gallowwood but built to define Wands culture wherever it recurs, this quest
is a craft rivalry rather than a fight — loud, enthusiastic, hands-first, exactly as
Wands culture is written. A master woodcarver and her upstart apprentice are at each
other's throats over who really made a piece both worked on, and the Fool's task is to
mediate a dispute that can only be settled the way Wands culture settles everything:
by the work itself, finished, side by side.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** Master carver Sorrel Ashgrove (canon, `characters.md` §Regional named NPCs) has entered a "trial piece" — a carved
   canopy-totem — into the Gallowwood's informal guild reckoning, a once-a-generation
   showcase of the region's finest work. Her apprentice, Fenn Cobblewright (canon, `characters.md` §Regional named NPCs),
   insists loudly, to anyone who'll listen, that he carved the totem's best sections
   himself and Sorrel is claiming sole credit.
2. **The complication.** Both are telling the truth as they understand it: Sorrel taught
   every technique Fenn used and considers guidance equivalent to authorship (a very
   Wands belief — the craft passes hand to hand, and the teacher's hands are in every
   piece their student makes); Fenn considers the specific hours he spent carving to be
   what actually counts. Neither is willing to back down, and both are too passionate
   about the piece itself to notice they're about to wreck it arguing.
3. **The mediation.** The Fool can gather testimony from other Wands craftspeople
   (gathering opinions is played as a loud, cheerful, opinionated tour of workshop
   culture rather than a fetch errand — each NPC has a strong, specific, funny take), find
   physical evidence in the tool-marks themselves (a puzzle rewarding attention to craft
   detail), or simply put both of them back in front of the unfinished totem and refuse
   to leave until they finish it together.
4. **The ache and the laugh.** The dispute resolves not through a verdict but through
   the act of finishing the piece together — hands overlapping, old teaching-rhythm
   reasserting itself despite the argument, both of them too absorbed in getting the last
   join right to keep fighting. The laugh: they finish, step back, and immediately start
   arguing about whose *name* goes on the small brass plaque. The ache: underneath the
   noise, it's clear this argument is really about Fenn wanting, badly, to be seen as
   more than a student — and Sorrel, just as badly, wanting not to have to let go of him
   yet.
5. **Closing beat.** The guild reckoning credits the piece to both names, side by side,
   which satisfies nobody completely and everybody enough — a very Wands resolution.

# SQ-SWORDS-01 — A Matter of Form

## Introduction

Homed in the Bastion but built to define Swords culture wherever it recurs, this quest
replaces an expected duel with an argument — because Swords culture, per canon, values
intellect and discipline as much as the blade, and "argues in premises" as a matter of
course. A young duelist wants to challenge a retired champion for her old rank; the Fool
discovers the challenge rests on a technicality that makes the whole contest a matter of
logic before it's a matter of steel, and that resolving it with restraint is the more
disciplined act, not the lesser one.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** Page-turned-hopeful Isolde Vantage (canon, `characters.md` §Regional named NPCs) intends to formally challenge retired champion
   Ser Barrow Steel (canon, `characters.md` §Regional named NPCs) for the rank of Knight, citing an old Bastion code that
   permits challenge "on any point of provable superiority." She's read the code
   carefully; she has not read it carefully *enough*.
2. **The complication.** The Fool, asking around, learns from a garrison clerk that the
   code's "provable superiority" clause was written to cover swordsmanship *or* argument
   — Ser Steel, decades retired from the blade, has spent his later years winning every
   challenge put to him purely on the strength of premises, never once drawing steel.
   Isolde doesn't know this; she's walking into a duel she can't win with the only weapon
   she's trained.
3. **The choice.** The Fool can warn Isolde and help her prepare an argument instead of a
   swing (Swords discipline as intellectual training, a montage of premises and
   counter-premises rather than blade drills), let the formal challenge play out and
   watch Ser Steel dismantle her case point by point with dry, respectful precision (no
   cruelty — Swords compliments land as observations, and his observations of her
   swordsmanship are genuinely admiring even as he wins), or find the actual loophole:
   the code technically permits a **conceded point** to end a challenge with honor equal
   to a won one.
4. **The ache and the laugh.** If Isolde loses outright, she takes it with real
   discipline — no tears, just a request to be taught the argument properly next time,
   which Ser Steel, visibly moved, agrees to on the spot (the ache: pride costs her
   something real; the laugh: his idea of "properly" starts at dawn, tomorrow, in the
   rain, because Swords culture never once considers comfort part of the lesson).
5. **Closing beat.** Isolde becomes Ser Steel's pupil rather than his rival — a small
   promotion of the theme that Swords honor is won through discipline sustained, not a
   single contest decided.

# SQ-LONGROAD-01 — What the Highwayman Left Behind

## Introduction

Once Justice lands its verdicts and the Assize queues disperse, three genuinely bad
actors walk free — and one of them, Gorrister Vale, comes back to the Longroad. He is
not robbing pilgrims this time; he is standing at a single milestone, the one where he
did his worst, unable to make himself either run or confess. The player finds a man the
Stall froze mid-crime for three centuries, so that "the highwayman" is now most of what
there is of him, and no self underneath it that knows how to be free. This quest does
not decide whether Vale has changed. It gives the player one concrete, un-grand thing to
do about the harm he left behind — restitution to one family he wronged — and lets his
redemption stay honestly his own to earn or fail, not the Fool's to declare for him.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** On the ring-causeway, Gorrister Vale (canon, `characters.md` §Recurring
   named NPCs) loiters at a weathered milestone, plainly waiting to be spoken to and
   plainly hoping no one will. Freed by `WS_JUSTICE_UNBOUND`, he owes nothing to anyone
   now — which is exactly why he can be pressed, and exactly why he can't be made to do
   anything. He robbed the dying: pilgrims bound for the Stillmarsh, taken on the one
   road they had left. This milestone is where he did the worst of it.
2. **The complication.** Vale won't perform contrition — he finds it as false as the
   player would. What he offers instead is a fact: a specific family, Coins-culture
   traders, lost a keepsake and a father's last purse to him here, and never got their
   reckoning because the trial that should have come never opened. He can name them. He
   will not walk to them.
3. **The errand.** The Fool carries the matter to the family down the Longroad (unnamed
   walk-ons). What Vale took is long spent, but the keepsake — a small, worthless-to-
   anyone-else thing — he kept, for reasons he can't articulate and won't be thanked for.
   The player returns it. The reunion is with an object, not a man.
4. **The ache.** The family's grief is old and does not lift because a stranger hands
   them a trinket; what mends is smaller — proof the wait meant something was owed, and
   that somebody finally carried it the last mile. Whether Vale is genuinely changed or
   simply between jobs is never resolved. He tells the Fool, dry and unpretty, that he
   doesn't know either.
   - Fool (earnest): "Give it back yourself." / (foolish): "Keep it. Run faster."
5. **The laugh.** Vale's one joke — that three hundred years frozen mid-robbery is the
   longest anybody's ever held up a coach — lands because he means it as a confession,
   not a boast.
6. **Closing beat.** Vale stays at his milestone or drifts back onto the road; the quest
   does not follow him to find out. Restitution done, the wronged family's future barks
   warm toward the Fool; Vale's do not change at all, which is the point.

## Open questions

- Should the wronged family be Coins-culture (traders, as sketched) or Cups-culture
  (hospitality, closer to the pilgrim framing)? Recommend Coins, so the restitution reads
  as a debt settled rather than a wound consoled.
- Does completing this quest change any bark from Vale, ever, or is his flat non-reaction
  the deliberate whole of it? Recommend the latter, per the "don't resolve them" rule.
- Should the returned keepsake carry a small tangible reward (a rose grafting held by the
  family as thanks), or stay Renown-only? Recommend Renown-only unless a grafting source
  is wanted here at content-pass.

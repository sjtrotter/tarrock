# SQ-CUPS-01 — The Guest-Right

## Introduction

Homed on the Divide's east bank but built to define Cups culture wherever it's
encountered again, this quest is a domestic dispute rather than a dungeon: two
households, one ancient grudge, and a suit-culture whose deepest law isn't spoken aloud
because everyone already knows it. Cups hospitality isn't a nicety here — it is close to
sacred, and the whole quest is a demonstration of what it costs and what it's worth to
take that seriously. The Fool arrives as an outsider with no stake in the grudge, which
is exactly why the quest works: only someone from nowhere can broker a peace this
particular and this stubborn.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** Goodwife Perrin Loosely (proposed NPC — promote to `characters.md`
   before script status), a widow on the east bank, has not let her oldest friend across
   her threshold in decades — since before the Stall, even — over a slight neither of
   them can now fully agree on the details of. By Cups suit-law, the **guest-right**:
   *any* properly-made request for shelter, food, or welcome must be honored, no
   exceptions, no grudges. Perrin has spent thirty years making sure her friend Ambrose
   Tench (proposed) never asks properly — deflecting, interrupting, changing the subject
   at the exact moment a real request might land.
2. **The complication.** The Fool learns the guest-right's actual ritual forms (a
   specific blessing-phrase, a specific gesture with an offered cup) from a Cups elder
   and realizes Ambrose has been asking wrong for decades — out of pride, not ignorance,
   since asking correctly would mean admitting how much he wants to be let back in. Both
   parties are equally, stubbornly complicit in the standoff continuing.
3. **The engineered moment.** The Fool can coach Ambrose through the proper asking, or
   coach Perrin through recognizing an improper ask as one anyway (the guest-right's
   spirit over its letter), or simply stage a scene — a shared meal, a toast — that
   traps them both inside their own culture's manners until the door opens whether they
   meant it to or not.
4. **The ache and the laugh.** Whichever route is taken, the reunion is not a tearful
   resolution of the old grudge — the slight itself is *never* actually resolved, and
   both of them privately admit, separately, to the Fool that they still think they were
   right. What's mended is smaller and truer: the door is open again. Perrin makes tea
   she is furious about having to make well. That is the whole reconciliation, and per
   Cups culture, it is enough.
5. **Closing beat.** Perrin's future greeting barks soften; Ambrose becomes a recurring
   presence at her hearth, bickering exactly as before. Nothing about the grudge is
   settled. Everything that mattered is.

# SQ-BASTION-02 — The Clerk Who Loved the Schedule

## Introduction

Freedom, this quest insists, is not universally wanted. With the Emperor unbound, the
Bastion's writs no longer stamp themselves to the gong — each now needs a person to
*decide*. For most citizens this is liberation. For junior clerk Pell Ashgrove, whose
whole life's comfort was a day with no decisions in it, his first discretionary case is
quietly the most frightening moment of his life. The Fool can talk him through it or
simply stand by while he manages alone; either way it is a small, unglamorous act of a
man becoming a person.

## Beats

1. **The hook.** The gong is silent for the first time in 300 years. At his desk, Pell
   Ashgrove (characters.md §Regional named NPCs) sits frozen before a single writ,
   because the rota that used to make every choice for him is gone and the choice is now
   his. He has stamped to a rhythm his whole career; there is no rhythm left to hide in.
2. **The case.** Genuinely small: two guilds claim the same delivery slot, both partly
   right. The old law would have decided by rota; the rota is dead. There is no correct
   answer now, only a chosen one — and to Pell a chosen answer is an abyss.
3. **Freedom isn't wanted (theme 3).** Pell explains, without a scrap of self-pity, that
   a life with no decisions was the kindest thing the Bastion ever gave him — and that
   everyone calling this "freedom" has not stopped to think what freedom asks of a man
   who never once wanted it.
4. **Offices eat people (theme 2).** Asked who he is apart from the stamp and the gong,
   Pell finds he has no ready answer. The office wore the man down to a hand and a rota;
   the quest is the hand learning it belongs to someone.
5. **The choice.** The Fool can walk Pell through the reasoning — weighing both claims
   aloud, teaching him that a fair-enough answer *chosen* beats a perfect one deferred
   forever — or say nothing and simply stay while he sweats it out himself. Both routes
   end the same way; only who did the deciding changes.
6. **The stamp / the laugh.** Pell finally stamps — hand shaking, ink blotted, the least
   tidy stamp of his career — and stares at it as if he has committed a crime. Then the
   small joke: he asks whether it can be *un*-stamped. It cannot. Nobody's can. He decides
   he is glad, mostly. [If CONFESSED: he asks whether a world that's ending even needs its
   paperwork decided, and answers himself that it does — because the deciding is the
   point, not the paper.]

Rewards, if any: modest coins; a small rise in the local suit's Renown.
